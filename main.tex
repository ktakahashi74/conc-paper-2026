\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Conchordal}, a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition whose sonic agents are governed by artificial life dynamics within a psychoacoustic fitness landscape. The system is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a design principle requiring that generative dynamics operate directly within a landscape derived from psychoacoustic observables. The environment integrates roughness and harmonicity into a continuous consonance field without presupposing scales or harmonic rules. Agents adjust pitch through local proposal-and-accept dynamics under a crowding penalty, regulate survival via consonance-dependent metabolism, and entrain temporally through Kuramoto-style phase coupling. Four experiments are reported: (1)~populations self-organize into structured polyphony with enriched consonant intervals; (2)~consonance-dependent metabolism yields survival differentials that vanish when recharge is disabled; (3)~a shared oscillatory scaffold organizes rhythmic timing under external forcing; (4)~hereditary pitch inheritance drives polyphonic convergence through differential survival alone. An integration test shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields a consonance gain exceeding either alone; the interaction vanishes on a shuffled landscape and generalizes across three landscape topologies. A supplementary mechanism check reports one possible composer-configurable bridge by which spectral state can modulate temporal coupling. These findings show that a psychoacoustically derived landscape serves as an effective artificial-life terrain, yielding self-organization, selection, synchronization, and hereditary adaptation in a non-traditional computational medium.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Artificial life has traditionally unfolded in spatial, chemical, or symbolic domains \citep{langton1989,bedau2000}. Yet sound---despite its rich physical structure and deep entanglement with biological perception---remains underexplored as a primary computational medium for life-like dynamics. Conchordal is a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition that treats consonance as an ecological resource within a continuous psychoacoustic field inhabited by autonomous agents.

Conchordal is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a design principle for perceptually grounded generative systems \citep{conchordal2025}: generative dynamics should operate directly within a landscape whose primary axes are derived from quantitative models of perceptual processing, without symbolic intermediaries. In Conchordal, cochlear roughness and periodicity-based harmonicity define a continuous consonance landscape; agents inhabit this perceptual geometry, and their ecological outcomes---survival, reproduction, synchronization---are determined by landscape position.

The present study evaluates whether such a system exhibits core artificial life properties. The psychoacoustic landscape and its attractor structure are established in \S2. Four experiments then test self-organization under environmental constraints (Experiment~1), selection mediated by resource dynamics (Experiment~2), synchronization through external forcing (Experiment~3), and hereditary adaptation through lineage-based pitch inheritance (Experiment~4).

\subsection*{Related Work}

\textbf{Sonic artificial life.}
Agent-based music generation spans swarm improvisation \citep{blackwell2002}, evolutionary sonic ecosystems \citep{mccormack2003}, spectral-energy recycling \citep{eldridge2009}, rule-based autonomous composition \citep{eigenfeldt2013}, and perceptually motivated harmony navigation \citep{bernardes2016}; see \citet{miranda2002} for a broader survey. These systems incorporate acoustic or perceptual features into agent design, but the generative substrate---the space agents inhabit and the fitness they optimize---is not derived from a quantitative perceptual model. DCC addresses this gap.

\textbf{Computational consonance.}
Beyond the classical Plomp--Levelt roughness model \citep{plomp1965} and Terhardt's virtual-pitch framework \citep{terhardt1979}, recent work has advanced periodicity-based consonance measures \citep{stolzenburg2015} and data-driven approaches using deep neural networks \citep{harrison2020}. Conchordal adopts a first-principles approach: roughness and harmonicity are computed from cochlear and periodicity models and combined through a bilinear consonance core, keeping the landscape interpretable and free of trained parameters.

\textbf{Embodied and predictive cognition.}
DCC resonates with embodied music cognition \citep{leman2007} and predictive processing \citep{clark2013}, both grounding perception and generation in shared structures. DCC operationalises this for instrument design: agents and listeners inhabit the same perceptual geometry.

\section{The Psychoacoustic Landscape}

Unlike the tunably rugged landscapes common in artificial life \citep{wright1932,kauffman1993}, Conchordal's landscape is derived deterministically from physical acoustics and cochlear mechanics, not from arbitrary fitness functions.

\subsection{Log-Frequency Representation}

All spectral relations are computed in $x = \log_2(f)$ space, where frequency ratios become additive displacements ($\Delta x = \log_2(f_2/f_1)$). This translational invariance lets harmonic templates and interference kernels be defined as convolutions, and is essential for modelling perceptual similarity structured by integer ratios \citep{glasberg1990}. For readability, intervals and distances are reported in cents (ct); $1200$\,ct $= 1$\,octave $= 1$ unit in $x$ space ($1$\,semitone $= 100$\,ct).

\subsection{Harmonicity}

Harmonicity $H(x)$ quantifies how well spectral energy at position $x$ in $\log_2$-frequency space is explained by a harmonic template \citep{terhardt1979,parncutt1988}. Periodicity extraction---the neural basis of virtual pitch---is well localized in the auditory brainstem and primary auditory cortex, making $H$ a relatively low-level quantity (though its perceptual weighting relative to roughness varies across cultures; see Limitations).

Computation proceeds by sibling projection, a two-pass convolution with power-law weights $w_k=k^{-\rho}$ ($\rho>0$):
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Roots}(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} S(x+\log_2 k)\;k^{-\rho},
      \label{eq:h-down}\\
  H(x) &= \sum_{m=1}^{N} \mathrm{Roots}(x-\log_2 m)\;m^{-\rho}.
      \label{eq:h-up}
\end{align}
%
The downward pass (\ref{eq:h-down}) accumulates evidence for virtual roots; the upward pass (\ref{eq:h-up}) redistributes that evidence to harmonics. For two tones at frequency ratio $p\!:\!q$, coincident harmonics accumulate constructively; the number of such coincidences within order $N$ grows as $\max(p,q)$ shrinks, so peaks at simpler integer ratios are systematically stronger (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}A).

In all experiments $N=16$ and $\rho=0.4$. Overtone and undertone projections are blended with equal weight. The field is normalized to $H_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Roughness}

Roughness $R(x)$ quantifies sensory dissonance from unresolved beating within the critical band \citep{plomp1965}. Like harmonicity, roughness reflects early peripheral processing: it arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane, a mechanism rooted in cochlear mechanics \citep{glasberg1990}.

The computation maps the amplitude spectrum $S$ onto the ERB-rate scale
%
\begin{equation}
  z = 21.4\,\log_{10}(0.00437\,f+1)
  \label{eq:erb}
\end{equation}
%
and convolves with a Plomp--Levelt interference kernel:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\mathrm{raw}}(z) = \int S(\zeta)\;g_{\mathrm{PL}}(|z-\zeta|)
      \,d\zeta,
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
%
where $g_{\mathrm{PL}}$ peaks at approximately $0.25$\,ERB and vanishes at both zero separation (no self-interference) and wide separation (outside the critical band). At simple integer ratios $p\!:\!q$, harmonics are spaced well beyond the critical band, producing roughness troughs that complement the harmonicity peaks (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}B).

The raw output is normalized via a reference-based saturation map to $R_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Consonance}

Unlike harmonicity and roughness, consonance is not a single well-defined perceptual quantity; it recruits higher-order circuits and is shaped by enculturation \citep{mcdermott2016}. No unique mapping $C(H,R)$ can be stipulated on purely psychoacoustic grounds. Because Conchordal uses consonance for landscape evaluation, metabolic signaling, and proposal weighting, we adopt a bilinear consonance core---the most general bilinear form for two $[0,1]$ inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
C = a\,H_{01} + b\,R_{01} + c\,H_{01}\,R_{01} + d.
\label{eq:consonance-core}
\end{equation}
%
Here $b<0$ penalizes roughness, while the interaction term $c$ modulates the roughness penalty conditionally on harmonicity. Different coefficient settings yield task-specific quantities from the same functional family.

The evaluation field for local pitch adaptation uses $(a,b,c,d)=(1,-1.35,1,0)$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{field}} = H_{01} - 1.35\,R_{01} + H_{01}\,R_{01}.
\end{equation}

A second parameterisation (setting $b\!=\!0,\,c\!=\!-1$),
%
\begin{equation}
  C_{\text{density}} = H_{01}(1 - R_{01}),
  \label{eq:c-density}
\end{equation}
%
provides a nonnegative mass used in Conchordal's interactive mode for spawn-position weighting; it is not used in the experiments reported here but is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}D for comparison. Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}C,D illustrate the resulting landscape for a single-anchor scan; both views exhibit attractor structure near simple integer ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3, 5:4), consistent with classical observations on consonance \citep{helmholtz1877,sethares1993}. No discrete scale was imposed; the attractors emerge solely from psychoacoustic structure.

For bounded metabolic control, $C_{\text{field}}$ is passed through a sigmoid: $C_{\text{level01}} = \sigma\!\bigl(\beta(C_{\text{field}}-\theta)\bigr)\in[0,1]$, with $(\beta,\theta)=(2,0)$.

\section{Agents and Dynamics}

The Conchordal instrument is designed around an extensible agent architecture that accommodates diverse behavioural strategies---including stochastic candidate sampling, movement costs, and exploration--persistence trade-offs---without altering the underlying landscape. The experiments reported here deliberately adopt minimal dynamics to isolate the landscape's contribution to self-organisation from strategy-specific effects; each mechanism described below is a simplification of the corresponding instrument subsystem.

\subsection{Pitch Adaptation}
\label{sec:pitch-adapt}

Each agent proposes local pitch perturbations of magnitude $\lambda=15$\,ct sampled within a bounded neighborhood in log-frequency space. A hill-climbing acceptance rule evaluates candidate positions via $C_{\text{field}}$ and a crowding penalty computed in ERB space. The crowding kernel $K_{\text{crowd}}(\Delta_{\text{erb}})$ is derived analytically as the complement of the roughness kernel: $K_{\text{crowd}}(d) = \max\!\bigl(0,\; 1 - R(d/\kappa) / R_{\text{peak}}\bigr)$, where $R$ is the full Plomp--Levelt roughness kernel and $\kappa$ is a reach factor ($\kappa=2.1$). This construction repels agents that are too close in pitch---preventing collapse to unison---while yielding to landscape-mediated repulsion at wider separations where roughness peaks. The combined score is $S = C_{\text{field}} - \lambda_C \sum_{j \neq i} K_{\text{crowd}}(\Delta_{ij})$. Proposals are accepted with probability $\min\!\bigl(1,\,\exp\bigl(\Delta S/T\bigr)\bigr)$ where $\Delta S = S_{\text{new}} - S_{\text{old}}$ and $T$ decays exponentially ($T_0=0.05$, $\tau=30$\,steps), annealing toward greedy hill-climbing.

\subsection{Metabolic Policy}

Agents possess energy $E$. Dynamics follow:

\begin{equation}
E \leftarrow E - c_b \Delta t - c_a I_{\text{attack}} + r_E \cdot C_{\text{level01}} \cdot I_{\text{attack}},
\end{equation}

where $c_b$ is basal cost, $c_a$ attack cost, $r_E$ recharge coefficient, and $I_{\text{attack}}\in\{0,1\}$ indicates a sound-onset event ($I_{\text{attack}}=1$ when the agent's phase oscillator crosses zero with $E>0$). Agents start with $E=1.0$ and perish when $E\leq 0$. We set $c_b=0.5$/s, $c_a=0.02$ per event, and $r_E=0.5$ per attack; time advances in hops of $512$ samples at $48\,$kHz ($\Delta t\approx 10.7\,$ms). In the baseline condition, recharge scales with consonance ($r_E=0.5$); in the ablation condition, $r_E=0$. An agent's \emph{vitality} $v = \sqrt{E/E_{\text{cap}}}$ (where $E_{\text{cap}}=1.0$) provides a normalised vigour signal available to downstream dynamics such as phase coupling.

\subsection{Temporal Oscillation}

Neural oscillations in auditory cortex entrain to periodic acoustic stimuli across multiple frequency bands \citep{lakatos2008,doelling2015}, and computational models of beat tracking posit coupled-oscillator dynamics as the underlying mechanism \citep{large1999}. Conchordal's full implementation realises this temporal axis of DCC through a multi-band modulation bank spanning delta through beta frequencies ($\approx$0.5--30\,Hz), mirroring layered temporal processing in cortical auditory networks. Agents couple to this shared oscillatory field rather than directly to one another (mean-field approximation), and the interaction is bidirectional: the field entrains agent timing while agent onsets excite the field.

The experiments extract the core coupling law as a single Kuramoto phase oscillator per agent:
%
\begin{equation}
\dot{\phi}_i = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N}\sum_j \sin(\phi_j - \phi_i) + F(t),
\end{equation}
%
where $K$ is coupling strength and $F(t)$ an external forcing term. Neural entrainment motivates the temporal axis itself, but current neuroscience does not uniquely determine how spectral state should modulate temporal coupling: harmonicity, attention, predictive context, and higher-order control all provide plausible links. Conchordal therefore treats spectral-to-temporal bridge mappings as composer-configurable design choices rather than fixed neurocognitive laws. Experiment~3 tests the shared oscillatory scaffold directly; one vitality-gated bridge used in the current implementation is documented separately as a supplementary mechanism check. Phase locking is quantified via the Kuramoto order parameter $R(t)$ \citep{kuramoto1984,strogatz2000} and phase-locking value (PLV) \citep{lachaux1999}.

\subsection{Experiment~1: Self-Organized Polyphony}

Twenty-four adaptive voices plus one immobile drone at 220\,Hz are placed on a discrete log-frequency grid (0.0025\,oct bins) spanning $\pm 2$ octaves from the drone. In the \emph{local-search} condition, agents generate local pitch proposals and accept them according to an exact leave-one-out (LOO) objective combining consonance with a crowding penalty. The \emph{random-walk} ablation keeps the same schedule, step window, and pitch bounds but replaces this score-based local search with a matched random local walk. LOO consonance is evaluated by removing each agent's spectral contribution from the shared environment before rescoring---an exact LOO that the real-time instrument approximates with a lighter-weight harmonic-projection subtraction. The LOO mean of $C_{\text{field}}$ across all agents defines $C_{\text{score}}$, the primary outcome metric.

Each run consists of 50 sweeps: a dissonance-seeking phase (steps 0--24) followed by a consonance-seeking phase (steps 25--49); the dissonance phase disperses agents before convergence, and the switch is implemented by negating the same exact-LOO proposal score ($S \to -S$). A consonance-only control (Supplementary S7) confirms that structure also emerges without this curriculum, though with significantly fewer stable pitch positions ($5.9$ vs.\ $9.4$ unique bins; $p < 0.0001$). Two conditions were compared (Figures~\ref{fig:e2-dynamics}--\ref{fig:e2-intervals}).

\textbf{Diversity.} Unique pitch bins averaged $9.4 \pm 1.2$ (\emph{local-search}) vs.\ $22.5 \pm 1.2$ (\emph{random-walk}). Under the crowding penalty and two-phase curriculum, score-based local search concentrates 24 agents into $\approx$\,9 distinct pitch classes at consonant intervals, whereas random walks distribute agents nearly uniformly across the grid. The crowding penalty is essential for preventing collapse to unison, while the dissonance-seeking phase broadens the pitch distribution (see Supplementary~S7). Scene consonance $G(F)=aH_{\mathrm{soc}}+bR_{\mathrm{soc}}+cH_{\mathrm{soc}}R_{\mathrm{soc}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:consonance-core}), where $H_{\mathrm{soc}}$ and $R_{\mathrm{soc}}$ denote the scene harmonicity and roughness after subtracting the mean singleton contribution, confirms the same dynamic: a sharp rise after the phase switch for \emph{local-search}, while \emph{random-walk} remains flat (Figure~\ref{fig:e2-dynamics}B).

\textbf{Interval Structure.} Shannon entropy of the pairwise interval distribution (240 bins of 5\,ct over 0--1200\,ct; uniform maximum $\ln 240 = 5.48$\,nats) was $1.57 \pm 0.35$\,nats (\emph{local-search}) vs.\ $4.97 \pm 0.04$ (\emph{random-walk}). The \emph{local-search} distribution concentrates mass at a small number of just-intonation intervals (Figure~\ref{fig:e2-intervals}A), whereas \emph{random-walk} approaches a near-uniform distribution. An independent just-intonation proximity score ($\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}}$; defined in \S\ref{sec:e6}) confirms the result: \emph{local-search} $0.453 \pm 0.039$ vs.\ \emph{random-walk} $0.105 \pm 0.013$ ($p < 0.0001$).

These results show that score-based local search is the primary source of structured polyphony: replacing it with matched random-walk updates disperses agents broadly and raises interval entropy toward the uniform ceiling. A shuffled-landscape control (Discussion, \S Terrain Validity) further confirms that this structure depends on coherent psychoacoustic gradients: destroying spatial coherence while preserving the marginal score distribution lowers post-switch $C_{\text{score}}$ from $0.731$ to $0.459$ and raises interval entropy from $1.52$ to $3.36$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$).

\subsection{Experiment~2: Consonance as Selection Pressure}

Each run maintained agents within $\pm 1$ octave of the 220\,Hz anchor; dead agents were immediately respawned at random positions until 200 deaths accumulated ($\approx$4{,}000 pooled lifetimes per condition across 20 seeds). Early consonance $C_{\mathrm{firstK}}$ (mean $C_{\text{level01}}$ over the first $K\!=\!20$ ticks) strongly predicted lifetime under baseline recharge (Figure~\ref{fig:e3-selection}). The primary analysis operates at the seed level to avoid inflated significance from pooling non-independent lifetimes within a run: seed-level analysis ($n\!=\!20$, Fisher $z$) yielded mean Pearson $r = 0.73$ (95\% CI $[0.69, 0.76]$; $p < 0.001$). Pooled ($n\!\approx\!4{,}000$) results are consistent: $r = 0.73$; median survival 371 vs.\ 339.

When recharge was disabled, seed-level mean $r = 0.003 \pm 0.08$, not significantly different from zero; the condition contrast was significant (Welch $t(27.0) = 22.2$, $p < 0.001$, Fisher $z$). Because pitch adaptation is disabled (Table~\ref{tab:conditions}), early consonance is determined entirely by initial position; the ablation confirms that the survival differential requires consonance-dependent recharge, not positional coincidence. Consonance thus functions as an ecological resource, generating selection pressure through the metabolic pathway.

\subsection{Experiment~3: Shared Rhythm Scaffold Organizes Timing}

Experiment~3 isolates the temporal axis of DCC directly. Rather than coupling synchrony to consonance or metabolic state, all agents are reduced to phase oscillators driven by an external 2\,Hz scaffold with fixed coupling strength ($K_{\mathrm{base}}\!=\!3.0$; intrinsic frequency $1.8\!\cdot\!2\pi$\,rad/s, $\pm 2\%$ jitter; 40\,s runs, 20 seeds each). This removes spectral confounds and tests the central temporal claim of the system: a shared oscillatory field should organize agent timing, whereas disrupting that shared temporal reference should weaken phase concentration.

Three conditions were compared. In \emph{shared}, all agents couple to the same continuous 2\,Hz phase reference. In \emph{scrambled}, the drive retains the same 2\,Hz cycle length and coupling magnitude, but its phase is re-randomized at each cycle boundary, destroying cross-cycle temporal continuity while preserving periodic energy. In \emph{off}, external drive coupling is disabled ($k_{\text{eff}} = 0$); agents retain intrinsic frequencies but receive no periodic forcing. All statistics are evaluated against the same canonical 2\,Hz beat, so the comparison measures stable beat alignment rather than condition-specific tracking of an idiosyncratic drive.

Figure~\ref{fig:e7-temporal}A shows that the shared-rhythm condition produces the strongest PLV to the canonical beat across the run. Figure~\ref{fig:e7-temporal}B averages onset-phase histograms across seeds: shared forcing yields a sharply concentrated phase distribution, whereas scrambled and off remain broadly dispersed. Figure~\ref{fig:e7-temporal}C summarizes seed-level onset vector strength and shows that scrambled still exceeds off when quantified across runs, even though their mean histograms are both diffuse relative to shared. The result is not that consonance uniquely determines rhythmic coupling; rather, it establishes that Conchordal's temporal axis is functional in its own right. A separate supplementary mechanism check reports one possible composer-configurable bridge in which consonance modulates vitality and thereby coupling strength, but this bridge is treated as an implementation option rather than a fixed neurocognitive law.

\subsection{Experiment~4: Hereditary Adaptation}
\label{sec:e6}

Experiments 1--3 establish selection but not heredity. To test whether lineage-based inheritance can consolidate ecological advantage, we compare two respawn conditions: \emph{heredity}, a lineage-biased respawn in which a dead agent's replacement pitch is drawn $\mathcal{N}(\text{random surviving parent},\,\sigma\!=\!0.003\,\log_2)$---a minimal inheritance mechanism analogous to cultural transmission rather than genetic encoding---versus \emph{random} uniform respawn within $\pm 1$ octave. Both conditions retain the crowding penalty but disable hill-climbing, isolating hereditary selection as the sole consonance-improvement pathway (remaining parameters in Table~\ref{tab:conditions}). Twenty seeds per condition were run until 4{,}000 deaths ($\approx 125$ turnover cycles).

Heredity runs yielded significantly higher final consonance than random controls ($C_{\text{score}} = 0.185 \pm 0.178$ vs.\ $0.061 \pm 0.018$; Welch $t=3.10$, $p=0.006$, Cohen's $d=0.98$; Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}). Pitch entropy dropped sharply under heredity ($1.65 \pm 0.39$ vs.\ $3.77 \pm 0.03$), indicating convergence toward specific pitch regions. The large inter-seed variance ($\pm 0.177$) reflects drift-dominated early dynamics: 8 of 20 heredity seeds showed $\Delta C > 0.1$ while 7 remained near baseline, consistent with stochastic founder effects \citep{kimura1983}. Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}B--D summarizes the same endpoint from complementary views: heredity leaves more voices inside the consonant windows and reduces their mean distance to the nearest consonant target relative to random respawn. This convergence arises purely from differential survival and inheritance: without hill-climbing, only the metabolic selection pressure identified in Experiment~2 drives the effect. Parameters were selected from a sweep over 15 conditions (Supplementary Table~S2); the reported setting is representative rather than the single best arm. To confirm that the consonance-score difference reflects genuine proximity to just-intonation ratios rather than an artefact of the in-simulation metric, we re-scored final configurations with an independent metric $\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}} = \binom{N}{2}^{-1}\!\sum_{i<j} \max_{p:q}\!\bigl[\tfrac{1}{pq}\,e^{-\alpha\,(\Delta_{ij}-r_{pq})^2}\bigr]$, the mean pairwise Gaussian proximity ($\alpha\!=\!4.0 \times 10^{-4}$\,ct$^{-2}$) to just-intonation ratios $p\!:\!q$ ($p,q \leq 8$), weighted by complexity $1/(pq)$: heredity scored substantially higher ($0.55 \pm 0.13$ vs.\ $0.11 \pm 0.01$; Welch $t=15.3$, $p<10^{-12}$, $d=4.8$).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Integrated Interpretation}

The four experiments form a cumulative argument. Self-organized polyphony (Experiment~1) shows that the landscape admits structured configurations beyond trivial collapse; metabolic selection (Experiment~2) validates that consonance-dependent recharge produces quantifiable survival differentials; temporal scaffolding (Experiment~3) shows that a shared oscillatory field can organize agent timing; and hereditary adaptation (Experiment~4) shows that selection pressure, once established, can accumulate across generations via inheritance alone. Each experiment isolates one mechanism; together they show that a psychoacoustic landscape can sustain the core ingredients of an artificial ecology without symbolic encoding. A supplementary mechanism check then documents one possible way the spectral and temporal axes can be linked in the current implementation, while leaving that bridge open as a configurable design choice rather than a uniquely validated law. As an integration test, we ran the Experiment~4 protocol under four conditions crossing heredity/random respawn with hill-climbing on/off (20 seeds each). Figure~\ref{fig:e6-integration} shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields $C_{\text{score}} = 0.53$, exceeding heredity-only ($0.19$), hill-only ($0.11$), and random ($0.06$). Because all four conditions share the same 20 random seeds, the interaction contrast is computed per-seed (HH$_i$ $-$ H$_i$ $-$ RH$_i$ $+$ R$_i$; mean $0.30$, 95\% CI $[0.20, 0.39]$; $p < 0.001$, sign-flip permutation), confirming that the combination exceeds the sum of individual effects. Repeating all four conditions on a shuffled landscape (Supplementary S8.4) eliminates the interaction entirely (contrast $0.03$, $p = 0.19$), confirming that the synergy requires coherent terrain structure.

\subsection{The Ecological--Aesthetic Duality}

In conventional generative music systems, ecological fitness and aesthetic evaluation are separate functions: an optimiser pursues one objective while a distinct critic judges the output. Because Conchordal's fitness landscape is derived directly from perceptual consonance, ecological viability and musical coherence are, by construction, the same quantity---a structural consequence of DCC. Each experiment can be read through this lens: self-organised polyphony (Experiment~1) is simultaneously an ecological equilibrium and a harmonic texture; metabolic selection (Experiment~2) is at once a survival differential and an aesthetic filter; shared rhythmic scaffolding (Experiment~3) shows that temporal order can be induced directly within the same ecology; and hereditary convergence (Experiment~4) is both an ecological adaptation and the formation of a harmonic tradition. The supplementary vitality-gated assay then illustrates one possible way a composer may route spectral state into that temporal scaffold without treating the mapping itself as fixed.

This duality is not merely a design convenience but a falsifiable structural claim: if the psychoacoustic landscape is ecologically functional, then ecological success should correlate with perceptual coherence---a prediction testable by behavioural listening experiments (see Limitations). In the absence of such independent perceptual validation, the duality remains a design hypothesis grounded in the structural correspondence between ecological fitness and psychoacoustic theory, rather than an empirically confirmed perceptual fact. Crucially, the duality is non-trivial: the terrain controls (\S Terrain Validity) confirm that destroying spatial coherence eliminates the structured outcomes while preserving ecological dynamics, demonstrating that the duality requires coherent psychoacoustic terrain structure, not merely the presence of a scalar fitness signal. A potential objection is that unison is ecologically optimal yet aesthetically trivial; the roughness and crowding penalties break this degeneracy (Experiment~1).

\subsection{Terrain Validity}

Because all in-simulation metrics derive from the same psychoacoustic model that defines the fitness landscape, a central question is whether the reported structure depends on the specific properties of that landscape or would arise under any scalar fitness field. To address this circularity, we introduced matched terrain controls (Supplementary~S8).
In a shuffled-landscape control, a fixed random permutation of the consonance-score bins---generated once per run and held fixed throughout all sweeps---was applied, preserving the marginal score distribution while destroying spatial coherence.
Pitch spread remained intact ($9.4$ vs.\ $9.6$ unique bins, $p = 0.57$), indicating that the crowding penalty alone prevents collapse, but interval structure degraded substantially: entropy rose from $1.52 \pm 0.36$ to $3.36 \pm 0.27$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$).
Thus, it is not merely the presence of a fitness signal that matters; a coherent psychoacoustic gradient is required for the emergence of structured polyphony.
The same terrain-dependence extends to selection: heredity benefits substantially from coherent terrain even without hill-climbing (Supplementary S8.4, $0.185$ vs.\ $0.051$, $p = 0.0002$), whereas random respawn shows no terrain dependence, confirming that the metabolic selection identified in Experiment~2 operates through heritable pitch structure.
A coefficient sweep over 16 $(b,c)$ kernel settings confirmed that polyphonic diversity is robust across the grid (Supplementary Table~S6).
The same control extends to the integrated system (as reported above; Supplementary S8.4). To test whether the interaction depends on specific landscape topology, we repeated the integration test under three environment-partials settings ($3$, $6$, $10$) producing qualitatively different harmonicity profiles. The interaction contrast remains large and significant across all topologies ($0.30$--$0.37$; $p < 0.001$; Supplementary S8.5), confirming that the synergy generalizes across landscape structures.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}

The ecological--aesthetic duality predicts that ecological success correlates with perceptual coherence, but the present work does not test this prediction against human judgements; behavioural listening experiments remain necessary. Because harmonicity--roughness weighting varies across listeners and cultures \citep{mcdermott2016}, the present coefficients implicitly encode a Western-leaning profile; listener-informed reparametrisation could enable non-Western perceptual weightings. All experiments use a fixed 220\,Hz reference; in Experiment~1 this reference is realised as an immobile drone voice. Including a fixed point is a compositional choice, not a system constraint: conventional instruments fix intonation at construction, whereas adaptive agents traverse pitch space continuously, so that removing the reference allows the emergent tuning itself to evolve during performance. Experiments with freely drifting populations and inharmonic timbres would test whether the present findings generalise to such conditions.

Experiment~4's hereditary adaptation converges to fixed-landscape attractors and does not constitute open-ended evolution \citep{bedau2000}; whether evolvable timbres or coupling topologies could support open-ended dynamics remains open. More broadly, closing the loop via real-time listener biosignals---feeding back brainwave or physiological signals to the landscape---would realise the bidirectional DCC envisioned in the Conchordal manifesto, turning perception and generation into a coupled dynamical system.

\section{Conclusion}

Conchordal demonstrates that a continuous landscape derived from psychoacoustic observables---harmonicity and roughness---can sustain the core ingredients of artificial ecology: self-organisation, selection, entrainment, and hereditary adaptation. Terrain controls confirm that these outcomes require the specific gradient structure of a perceptually grounded landscape, establishing DCC-derived consonance as a viable ALife substrate that complements the spatial and chemical media traditionally studied in the field.

A central consequence is the ecological--aesthetic duality: because the fitness landscape is derived from psychoacoustic models of auditory processing, ecological viability and musical coherence become structurally coupled. Survival, synchronisation, and hereditary convergence are simultaneously ecological processes and acts of music-making---no separate aesthetic criterion is required. As an instrument, Conchordal thus offers composers a generative substrate in which ecological dynamics produce musically coherent material without symbolic specification. More broadly, DCC is not inherently auditory: any creative medium whose perceptual basis admits quantitative modelling---visual, tactile, or kinesthetic---could host an analogous ecology.
