\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Conchordal}, a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition whose sonic agents are governed by artificial life dynamics within a psychoacoustic fitness landscape defined in log-frequency space. The system is built on the principle of \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC): the generative process operates directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries. The environment integrates roughness and harmonicity into a continuous consonance field without presupposing musical scales or harmonic rules. Agents adjust pitch through local proposal-and-accept dynamics under density-based repulsion, regulate survival via a consonance-dependent metabolic policy, and entrain temporally through Kuramoto-style phase coupling grounded in neural oscillatory models. Four experiments are reported. (1) Populations self-organize into structured, non-collapsed polyphony with enriched consonant interval distributions. (2) When consonance modulates metabolic recharge, early consonance strongly predicts lifetime, whereas the effect vanishes without recharge. (3) In a controlled entrainment assay, consonance-dependent vitality biases synchronization: agents at consonant positions synchronize more readily. (4) Hereditary pitch inheritance drives polyphonic convergence to multiple consonant ratios through differential survival alone. These findings demonstrate that consonance can serve as the organising resource for life-like dynamics in a non-traditional computational medium, yielding self-organization, selection, synchronization, and hereditary adaptation. Because DCC grounds the fitness landscape in perceptual structure, an ecological--aesthetic duality obtains: adaptive success and musical coherence become structurally aligned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Artificial life has traditionally unfolded in spatial, chemical, or symbolic domains \citep{langton1989,bedau2000}. Yet sound---despite its rich physical structure and deep entanglement with biological perception---remains underexplored as a primary computational medium for life-like dynamics. Conchordal is a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition that treats consonance as an ecological resource within a continuous psychoacoustic field inhabited by autonomous agents.

Conchordal is built on the principle of \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC): the generative process operates directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries \citep{conchordal2025}. Concretely, DCC imposes two requirements: (i)~the fitness landscape must be derived from quantitative models of perceptual processing (here, cochlear roughness and periodicity-based harmonicity), and (ii)~agent dynamics should, where possible, be grounded in the same perceptual or neural models rather than in domain-external heuristics. In the present system, criterion~(ii) is most fully realised for temporal coupling: the Kuramoto phase law is adopted because it is the model invoked by Dynamic Attending Theory for cortical beat tracking \citep{large1999}. Pitch repulsion and metabolic dynamics satisfy~(ii) partially: repulsion operates in ERB-scaled space (the coordinate system of cochlear filtering) and metabolic signals are driven by the perceptual consonance field, but the functional forms themselves (Gaussian kernel, linear energy drain) are engineering choices rather than neural-model derivations. Systems that employ a perceptual fitness function while using entirely arbitrary agent dynamics (e.g.\ genetic operators, swarm velocity rules) satisfy~(i) alone. In the auditory case, DCC entails dispensing with scales, metrical grids, and note durations: agents seek locally advantageous regions of a landscape shaped by harmonicity and roughness \citep{plomp1965,terhardt1979}, and consonance is ecological before it is aesthetic.

\subsection*{Related Work}

\textbf{Sonic artificial life.}
Several systems have explored agent-based music generation \citep{miranda2002}. \citet{blackwell2002} used particle swarm optimization for real-time improvisation, with agents navigating a feature space shaped by performer feedback rather than perceptual models. \citet{mccormack2003} constructed evolutionary ecosystems in which agents reproduce and mutate sonic material. \citet{eldridge2009} recycled spectral energy through a filter-based ecosystem. \citet{eigenfeldt2013} deployed rule-based autonomous agents for real-time composition. \citet{bernardes2016} navigated a perceptually motivated tonal interval space for harmony generation. These systems satisfy criterion~(i) to varying degrees---some use perceptually informed representations---but their agent dynamics (genetic operators, swarm velocities, rule-based sequencing) are not derived from perceptual or neural models. Conchordal advances toward both criteria, grounding the landscape in psychoacoustic structure and adopting neural-oscillatory coupling for temporal dynamics.

\textbf{Computational consonance.}
Beyond the classical Plomp--Levelt roughness model \citep{plomp1965} and Terhardt's virtual-pitch framework \citep{terhardt1979}, recent work has advanced periodicity-based consonance measures \citep{stolzenburg2015} and data-driven approaches using deep neural networks \citep{harrison2020}. Conchordal adopts a first-principles approach: roughness and harmonicity are computed from cochlear and periodicity models and combined through a bilinear consonance core, keeping the landscape interpretable and free of trained parameters.

\textbf{Embodied and predictive cognition.}
DCC resonates with embodied music cognition \citep{leman2007} and predictive processing \citep{clark2013}, both of which ground perception in shared generative structures. DCC operationalises a similar principle: agents and listeners inhabit the same perceptual geometry.

The present study evaluates whether such a system exhibits core artificial life properties. The psychoacoustic landscape and its attractor structure are established in \S2. Four experiments then test self-organization under environmental constraints (Experiment~1), selection mediated by resource dynamics (Experiment~2), synchronization through external forcing (Experiment~3), and hereditary adaptation through lineage-based pitch inheritance (Experiment~4).

\section{The Psychoacoustic Landscape}

Unlike the tunably rugged landscapes common in artificial life \citep{wright1932,kauffman1993}, Conchordal's landscape is derived deterministically from physical acoustics and cochlear mechanics, not from arbitrary fitness functions.

\subsection{Log-Frequency Representation}

All spectral relations are computed in $x = \log_2(f)$ space, where frequency ratios become additive displacements ($\Delta x = \log_2(f_2/f_1)$). This translational invariance lets harmonic templates and interference kernels be defined as convolutions, and is essential for modelling perceptual similarity structured by integer ratios \citep{glasberg1990}.

\subsection{Harmonicity}

Harmonicity $H(x)$ quantifies how well spectral energy at position $x$ in log$_2$-frequency space is explained by a harmonic template \citep{terhardt1979,parncutt1988}. Periodicity extraction---the neural basis of virtual pitch---is well localized in the auditory brainstem and primary auditory cortex, making $H$ a relatively low-level quantity (though its perceptual weighting relative to roughness varies across cultures; see \S\ref{sec:limits}).

Computation proceeds by sibling projection, a two-pass convolution with power-law weights $w_k=k^{-\rho}$ ($\rho>0$):
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Roots}(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} S(x+\log_2 k)\;k^{-\rho},
      \label{eq:h-down}\\
  H(x) &= \sum_{m=1}^{N} \mathrm{Roots}(x-\log_2 m)\;m^{-\rho}.
      \label{eq:h-up}
\end{align}
%
The downward pass (\ref{eq:h-down}) accumulates evidence for virtual roots; the upward pass (\ref{eq:h-up}) redistributes that evidence to harmonics. For two tones at frequency ratio $p\!:\!q$, coincident harmonics accumulate constructively; the number of such coincidences within order $N$ grows as $\max(p,q)$ shrinks, so peaks at simpler integer ratios are systematically stronger (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}A).

In all experiments $N=16$ and $\rho=0.4$. Overtone and undertone projections are blended with equal weight; full parameterization is given in \citet{conchordal2025}. The field is normalized to $H_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Roughness}

Roughness $R(x)$ quantifies sensory dissonance from unresolved beating within the critical band \citep{plomp1965}. Like harmonicity, roughness reflects early peripheral processing: it arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane, a mechanism rooted in cochlear mechanics \citep{glasberg1990}.

The computation maps the amplitude spectrum $S$ onto the ERB-rate scale $z = 21.4\log_{10}(0.00437f+1)$ and convolves with a Plomp--Levelt interference kernel:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\mathrm{raw}}(z) = \int S(\zeta)\;g_{\mathrm{PL}}(|z-\zeta|)
      \,d\zeta,
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
%
where $g_{\mathrm{PL}}$ peaks at approximately $0.25$\,ERB and vanishes at both zero separation (no self-interference) and wide separation (outside the critical band). At simple integer ratios $p\!:\!q$, harmonics are spaced well beyond the critical band, producing roughness troughs that complement the harmonicity peaks (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}B).

The raw output is normalized via a reference-based saturation map to $R_{01}\in[0,1]$; details are given in \citet{conchordal2025}.

\subsection{Consonance}

Unlike harmonicity and roughness, consonance is not a single well-defined perceptual quantity; it recruits higher-order circuits and is shaped by enculturation \citep{mcdermott2016}. No unique mapping $C(H,R)$ can be stipulated on purely psychoacoustic grounds. Because Conchordal uses consonance for landscape evaluation, metabolic signaling, and proposal weighting, we adopt a bilinear consonance core---the most general bilinear form for two $[0,1]$ inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
C = a\,H_{01} + b\,R_{01} + c\,H_{01}\,R_{01} + d.
\label{eq:consonance-core}
\end{equation}
%
Here $b<0$ penalizes roughness, while the interaction term $c$ modulates the roughness penalty conditionally on harmonicity. Different coefficient settings yield task-specific quantities from the same functional family.

The evaluation field for local pitch adaptation uses $(a,b,c,d)=(1,-1.35,1,0)$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{field}} = H_{01} - 1.35\,R_{01} + H_{01}\,R_{01}.
\end{equation}

For density-based proposal weighting, we set $(a,b,c,d)=(1,0,-1,0)$, yielding a dedicated nonnegative mass:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{density}} = H_{01}(1 - R_{01}).
\end{equation}

This separation avoids conflating acceptance utility ($C_{\text{field}}$) with proposal density ($C_{\text{density}}$). Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}C,D illustrates the resulting landscape for a single-anchor scan; both views exhibit attractor structure near simple integer ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3, 5:4), consistent with classical observations on consonance \citep{helmholtz1877,sethares1993}. No discrete scale was imposed; the attractors emerge solely from psychoacoustic structure.

For bounded metabolic control, $C_{\text{field}}$ is passed through a sigmoid:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{level01}} = \sigma\!\bigl(\beta(C_{\text{field}}-\theta)\bigr)\in[0,1], \quad (\beta,\theta)=(2,0).
\end{equation}

\section{Agents and Dynamics}

\subsection{Pitch Adaptation}
\label{sec:pitch-adapt}

Each agent proposes local pitch perturbations of magnitude $\lambda=0.15$\,st sampled within a bounded neighborhood in log-frequency space. A hill-climbing acceptance rule evaluates candidate positions via $C_{\text{field}}$ and a density-based repulsion term. Repulsion is computed through a Gaussian kernel ($\sigma=0.06$\,st) over pitch distances, discouraging collapse to identical frequencies. Proposals are accepted stochastically with a temperature that decays exponentially ($T_0=0.05$, $\tau=30$\,steps), annealing toward greedy hill-climbing.

Ablation conditions remove either the hill-climbing mechanism or the repulsion term.

\subsection{Metabolic Policy}

Agents possess energy $E$. Dynamics follow:

\begin{equation}
E' = E - c_b \Delta t - c_a I_{\text{attack}} + r_E \cdot C_{\text{level01}} \cdot I_{\text{attack}},
\end{equation}

where $c_b$ is basal cost, $c_a$ attack cost, and $r_E$ recharge coefficient. Agents start with $E=1.0$ and perish when $E\leq 0$. We set $c_b=0.5$/s, $c_a=0.02$ per event, and $r_E=0.5$ per attack; time advances in hops of $512$ samples at $48\,$kHz ($\Delta t\approx 10.7\,$ms). In the baseline condition, recharge scales with consonance ($r_E=0.5$); in the ablation condition, $r_E=0$.

\subsection{Temporal Oscillation}

Each agent contains a phase oscillator:

\begin{equation}
\dot{\phi_i} = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N}\sum_j \sin(\phi_j - \phi_i) + F(t).
\end{equation}

Here $K$ is coupling strength and $F(t)$ is an optional external forcing term (``kick''). Phase locking is quantified via the Kuramoto order parameter $R(t)$ \citep{kuramoto1984,strogatz2000} and phase-locking value (PLV) \citep{lachaux1999}.

The Kuramoto model realises the temporal axis of DCC: cortical oscillations entrain to periodic auditory stimuli \citep{lakatos2008,doelling2015}, and neural phase-locking predicts perceived rhythmic salience \citep{nozaradan2011}. Agent timing thus shares the coupling law hypothesised for cortical beat tracking \citep{large1999}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Self-Organized Polyphony}

Populations of 24 agents were simulated for 100 pitch-proposal rounds (the first 10 rounds are discarded as burn-in) across 20 seeds per condition. Three conditions were compared: baseline, no hill-climb, and no repulsion.

\textbf{Diversity.} Unique pitch bins averaged $19.2 \pm 1.4$ (baseline) versus $13.1 \pm 1.3$ (no repulsion; Welch $t=14.5$, $p<10^{-13}$, Cohen's $d=4.6$). Mean nearest-neighbor distance was $0.57 \pm 0.05$\,st (baseline), dropping to $0.29 \pm 0.11$\,st under no-repulsion ($t=11.0$, $p<10^{-11}$, $d=3.5$), confirming pitch collapse.

\textbf{Interval Structure.} Shannon entropy of the pairwise interval distribution (240 bins of 0.05\,st over 0--12\,st; uniform maximum $\ln 240 = 5.48$\,nats) was $3.95 \pm 0.17$ nats (baseline), rising to $4.92 \pm 0.06$ under no-hill-climbing ($t=-23.4$, $p<10^{-22}$, $d=7.4$) and falling to $3.57 \pm 0.29$ under no-repulsion ($t=5.1$, $p<10^{-4}$, $d=1.6$). The no-hill increase toward the uniform ceiling reflects a distribution lacking consonant peaks; the no-repulsion decrease reflects collapse toward fewer intervals.

These results show that hill-climbing enriches consonant interval structure, while repulsion prevents monophonic collapse. Self-organized harmonic distributions arise from the landscape--agent interaction.

\subsection{Consonance as Selection Pressure}

Experiments 2--4 increase population size to $N\!=\!32$. Each run maintained 32 agents within $\pm 1$ octave of a 220\,Hz anchor; dead agents were immediately respawned at new random positions. Runs continued until 200 deaths accumulated (1000 pooled lifetimes per condition across five seeds). Early consonance (first 20 ticks) strongly predicted lifetime under baseline recharge (Pearson $r = 0.71$; Spearman $\rho_s = 0.45$; log-rank $p < 0.001$). Median survival differed markedly between high- and low-consonance groups (372 vs.\ 339). The effect was consistent across seeds (per-seed Pearson $r$ range $0.63$--$0.80$; all log-rank $p < 0.001$).

When recharge was disabled, correlations vanished (Pearson $r = 0.028$; log-rank $p = 0.33$). Because agents do not move, early consonance is determined by initial pitch position; the recharge ablation confirms that the survival differential requires consonance-dependent energy replenishment, not merely positional coincidence. Consonance thus functions as an ecological resource, generating selection pressure through the metabolic pathway.

\subsection{Consonance-Gated Entrainment}

In Conchordal, consonance modulates metabolic energy, which determines vitality, which can scale the strength of Kuramoto phase coupling. This chain predicts that agents at consonant pitch positions should synchronize more readily to an external rhythm than those at dissonant positions---a \emph{consonance-gated} entrainment effect under controlled conditions.

We test this with 32 agents whose pitches are assigned by stratified consonance sampling within $\pm 1$ octave of a 220\,Hz anchor (one pitch per equal-width consonance stratum). To isolate the consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway, we use a simplified continuous energy model (omitting the attack-gated dynamics of \S3.2): $dE/dt = -c_b + r_E \cdot C_i$ ($c_b\!=\!0.05$/s, $r_E\!=\!0.1$/s---note the continuous-time units, distinct from the per-attack $r_E$ in \S3.2---, $E_{\mathrm{cap}}\!=\!1.0$). Vitality $v = \sqrt{E / E_{\mathrm{cap}}}$ scales coupling to a 2\,Hz external kick ($K_{\mathrm{base}}\!=\!3.0$; intrinsic frequency $1.8\!\cdot\!2\pi$\,rad/s, $\pm 2\%$ jitter). Three conditions: \emph{vitality} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot v_i$), \emph{uniform} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot \bar{v}$), and \emph{control} ($k_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$); 40\,s runs, 20 seeds each.

Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}A shows the group mean PLV over time. Both vitality and uniform conditions synchronize above the control baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}B plots per-agent final PLV against consonance (using stratified pitch assignment to span the full consonance range): in the vitality condition, consonant agents achieve near-unity PLV while dissonant agents remain desynchronized, whereas the control condition shows no such gradient. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}C summarizes the Pearson $r$(consonance, PLV) across all seeds. The vitality condition yields a strong positive correlation ($r = 0.75 \pm 0.09$), confirming that consonance position selectively modulates synchronization strength. The uniform condition, despite identical total coupling, shows near-zero correlation ($r \approx 0.0$), isolating the effect to per-agent vitality modulation rather than aggregate coupling magnitude. Note that the near-zero uniform correlation is by design: equalizing coupling strengths removes the channel through which consonance could influence synchronization, so the contrast serves to confirm, not merely demonstrate, the vitality-mediation pathway. This experiment is a pathway-isolation assay: it demonstrates one spectral-to-temporal bridge, not that rhythmic organization in the full runtime is determined by consonance alone.

\subsection{Hereditary Adaptation}

Experiments 1--3 establish selection but not heredity: agents survive differentially yet do not transmit traits to offspring. To test whether lineage-based inheritance can consolidate ecological advantage, we compare two respawn conditions. In the \emph{heredity} condition, a dead agent is replaced by an offspring whose pitch is drawn from a normal distribution centered on a randomly chosen surviving parent ($\sigma=0.003$\,log$_2$, i.e.\ $\approx 0.036$\,st). In the \emph{random} condition, offspring pitch is drawn uniformly within $\pm 1$ octave of the anchor. Both conditions use density-based perceptual repulsion (Experiment~1 established that repulsion is necessary for polyphonic diversity) but disable consonance-based hill-climbing (landscape weight $= 0$), so that the only mechanism for consonance improvement is hereditary selection through metabolic advantage. All other parameters---including the attack-gated metabolic model of \S3.2 (32 agents, 220\,Hz anchor, $r_E=0.5$)---match Experiment~2. Twenty seeds per condition were run until 4{,}000 deaths accumulated ($\approx 125$ turnover cycles, where one cycle $= N$ deaths).

Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary} shows that heredity runs produce a statistically significant increase in mean consonance relative to random controls. Welch's $t$-test confirmed higher consonance change in the heredity condition ($\Delta C_{\text{score}} = +0.121 \pm 0.177$ vs.\ $-0.003 \pm 0.027$; $t=3.10$, $p=0.001$, Cohen's $d=0.98$). Final consonance was likewise higher (heredity $0.185 \pm 0.178$ vs.\ random $0.061 \pm 0.018$; $t=3.13$, $p<0.001$). Pitch entropy dropped sharply under heredity ($1.67 \pm 0.46$ vs.\ $3.78 \pm 0.03$), indicating convergence toward specific pitch regions. The large inter-seed variance ($\pm 0.177$) reflects drift-dominated early dynamics: 8 of 20 heredity seeds showed $\Delta C > 0.1$ while 7 remained near baseline, consistent with stochastic founder effects \citep{kimura1983}. The final pitch histogram (Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}C) shows that heredity runs concentrate near consonant ratios rather than remaining uniformly dispersed. This convergence arises purely from differential survival and inheritance: without hill-climbing, only the metabolic selection pressure identified in Experiment~2 drives the effect. Mutation magnitude ($\sigma=0.003\,\log_2$) and generation count (4{,}000 deaths) were selected from a parameter sweep over 15 conditions ($\sigma \in \{0.001, 0.003, 0.01, 0.03, 0.1\} \times \text{deaths} \in \{1000, 4000, 8000\}$). The heredity--random separation was positive in 12 of 15 conditions and statistically significant ($p<0.05$) in 7; the reported setting was chosen for the clearest separation, but the qualitative effect is not confined to a narrow parameter window.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Integrated Interpretation}

The four experiments form a cumulative argument. Self-organized polyphony (Exp.~1) establishes that the landscape admits structured configurations beyond trivial collapse; metabolic selection (Exp.~2) shows that consonance can differentially filter agents; entrainment gating (Exp.~3) bridges the spectral and temporal axes through a single identified pathway; and hereditary adaptation (Exp.~4) demonstrates that selection pressure, once established, can accumulate across generations via inheritance alone. Each experiment isolates one mechanism; together they show that a psychoacoustic landscape can sustain the core ingredients of an artificial ecology without symbolic encoding. Studying the full system with all mechanisms active simultaneously---where interactions may produce dynamics not predicted by isolated experiments---is a natural next step.

\subsection{The Ecological--Aesthetic Duality}

Because the fitness landscape is derived from perceptual consonance, the conditions for ecological viability and for musical coherence are, by design, the same quantity. This is a structural consequence of DCC, not an empirical discovery: agents that survive occupy consonant regions; populations that self-organize produce harmonically enriched polyphony; oscillators that entrain generate perceptually salient rhythms. The duality is therefore guaranteed at the model level.

Its nontrivial content lies in the falsifiable predictions it generates. If DCC succeeds in its intended role, listeners should rate emergent configurations as more consonant than ablated controls, and cortical entrainment to DCC-generated rhythms should exceed that to non-DCC baselines (\S\ref{sec:limits}). In a system whose fitness landscape is arbitrary, ecological success and aesthetic coherence may coincide only by chance; DCC narrows that gap by construction, but whether the gap closes for human listeners remains an open empirical question.

A potential objection is that maximal consonance (unison) is ecologically optimal yet aesthetically trivial. Repulsion breaks this degeneracy: Experiment~1 shows that removing it collapses populations to a single pitch. The duality therefore holds for the full ecological dynamic---consonance \emph{plus} repulsion---not for consonance alone.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}

The claim that DCC-generated configurations are ``perceptually salient by construction'' has not yet been validated with human listeners. The DCC hypothesis yields testable predictions: (a)~emergent pitch configurations should receive higher consonance ratings than ablated controls; (b)~cortical entrainment (frequency-tagged EEG) to agent-generated rhythms should exceed non-DCC baselines. Until such validation is performed, the ecological--aesthetic duality remains a structural prediction.

The bilinear consonance core (\ref{eq:consonance-core}) introduces coefficients $(a,b,c,d)$ that have not been subjected to systematic sensitivity analysis. Because the weighting of harmonicity versus roughness varies across listeners and cultures \citep{mcdermott2016}, the present coefficients implicitly encode a Western-leaning profile. A parametric sweep across coefficient space would clarify robustness and enable exploration of non-Western perceptual weightings.

Experiment~4's hereditary adaptation converges to fixed-landscape attractors and does not constitute open-ended evolution \citep{bedau2000}; whether evolvable timbres or coupling topologies could support open-ended dynamics remains open.

The consonance landscape currently assumes harmonic spectra. Extending the roughness and harmonicity kernels to inharmonic or noise-bearing timbres would broaden the ecology to percussion, bells, and other non-pitched sound sources, testing whether DCC generalises beyond tonal contexts.

The present system realises the first stage of DCC: mapping from cognitive model to acoustic environment. A natural next stage is closing the loop via real-time listener biosignals (EEG, heart rate), turning perception and generation into a coupled dynamical system \citep{conchordal2025}.

\section{Conclusion}

Conchordal establishes a psychoacoustic artificial life environment grounded in Direct Cognitive Coupling. In log-frequency space, harmonicity and roughness define a continuous landscape supporting attractors, population-level structure, survival differentials, consonance-gated entrainment, and hereditary adaptation. These results suggest that the core ingredients of artificial ecology---self-organization, selection, cross-domain coupling, and hereditary adaptation---can be instantiated in an auditory medium structured by perceptual law, complementing the spatial and chemical substrates traditionally studied in ALife. As an instrument, Conchordal offers composers a generative substrate in which ecological dynamics produce musically coherent material without symbolic specification; informal listening suggests that baseline configurations yield recognisably consonant textures, though formal perceptual evaluation is deferred to future work.

More broadly, the DCC principle is not inherently auditory: any creative medium whose perceptual basis admits quantitative modelling could, in principle, host an analogous ecology \citep{conchordal2025}. Conchordal serves as a proof of concept for the auditory case; whether analogous dynamics arise in visual or tactile domains remains to be explored.
