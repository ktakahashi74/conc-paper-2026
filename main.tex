\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Conchordal}, a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition whose sonic agents are governed by artificial life dynamics within a psychoacoustic fitness landscape defined in log-frequency space. The system is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a design principle requiring that the generative process operate directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries. The environment integrates roughness and harmonicity into a continuous consonance field without presupposing musical scales or harmonic rules. Agents adjust pitch through local proposal-and-accept dynamics under density-based repulsion, regulate survival via a consonance-dependent metabolic policy, and entrain temporally through Kuramoto-style phase coupling grounded in neural oscillatory models. Four experiments are reported. (1) Populations self-organize into structured, non-collapsed polyphony with enriched consonant interval distributions. (2) When consonance modulates metabolic recharge, early consonance strongly predicts lifetime, whereas the effect vanishes without recharge. (3) In a controlled entrainment assay, consonance-dependent vitality biases synchronization: agents at consonant positions synchronize more readily. (4) Hereditary pitch inheritance drives polyphonic convergence to multiple consonant ratios through differential survival alone. A preliminary integration test shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields a consonance gain significantly exceeding either mechanism alone (interaction contrast $p < 0.001$, sign-flip permutation). A matched terrain control confirms that these outcomes require coherent psychoacoustic gradient structure, not merely the presence of a fitness signal: shuffling the landscape to destroy spatial coherence leaves pitch spread intact but raises interval entropy toward the uniform ceiling. These findings show that a psychoacoustically derived landscape can serve as an effective artificial-life terrain, yielding self-organization, selection, synchronization, and hereditary adaptation in a non-traditional computational medium.
\end{abstract}

Artificial life has traditionally unfolded in spatial, chemical, or symbolic domains \citep{langton1989,bedau2000}. Yet sound---despite its rich physical structure and deep entanglement with biological perception---remains underexplored as a primary computational medium for life-like dynamics. Conchordal is a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition that treats consonance as an ecological resource within a continuous psychoacoustic field inhabited by autonomous agents.

Conchordal is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a principle for perceptually grounded generative systems \citep{conchordal2025}: the generative process should operate directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries. Concretely, DCC imposes two design requirements: (i)~the fitness landscape must be derived from quantitative models of perceptual processing (here, cochlear roughness and periodicity-based harmonicity), and (ii)~agent dynamics should, where possible, be grounded in the same perceptual or neural models rather than in domain-external heuristics. In the present system, criterion~(ii) is most fully realized for temporal coupling: the Kuramoto phase law is adopted because it is the model invoked by Dynamic Attending Theory for cortical beat tracking \citep{large1999}. Pitch repulsion and metabolic dynamics satisfy~(ii) partially: repulsion operates in ERB-scaled space (the coordinate system of cochlear filtering) and metabolic signals are driven by the perceptual consonance field, but the functional forms themselves (Gaussian kernel, linear energy drain) are engineering choices rather than neural-model derivations. In the auditory case, DCC entails dispensing with scales, metrical grids, and note durations: agents seek locally advantageous regions of a landscape shaped by harmonicity and roughness \citep{plomp1965,terhardt1979}, and consonance is ecological before it is aesthetic.

\subsection*{Related Work}

\textbf{Sonic artificial life.}
Agent-based music generation spans swarm improvisation \citep{blackwell2002}, evolutionary sonic ecosystems \citep{mccormack2003}, spectral-energy recycling \citep{eldridge2009}, rule-based autonomous composition \citep{eigenfeldt2013}, and perceptually motivated harmony navigation \citep{bernardes2016}; see \citet{miranda2002} for a broader survey. These systems satisfy criterion~(i) to varying degrees, but their agent dynamics (genetic operators, swarm velocities, rule-based sequencing) are not derived from perceptual or neural models. Conchordal advances toward both criteria.

\textbf{Computational consonance.}
Beyond the classical Plomp--Levelt roughness model \citep{plomp1965} and Terhardt's virtual-pitch framework \citep{terhardt1979}, recent work has advanced periodicity-based consonance measures \citep{stolzenburg2015} and data-driven approaches using deep neural networks \citep{harrison2020}. Conchordal adopts a first-principles approach: roughness and harmonicity are computed from cochlear and periodicity models and combined through a bilinear consonance core, keeping the landscape interpretable and free of trained parameters.

\textbf{Embodied and predictive cognition.}
As a design principle, DCC resonates with embodied music cognition \citep{leman2007} and predictive processing \citep{clark2013}, both of which ground perception in shared generative structures. DCC operationalises a similar insight for instrument design: agents and listeners inhabit the same perceptual geometry.

The present study evaluates whether such a system exhibits core artificial life properties. The psychoacoustic landscape and its attractor structure are established in \S2. Four experiments then test self-organization under environmental constraints (Experiment~1), selection mediated by resource dynamics (Experiment~2), synchronization through external forcing (Experiment~3), and hereditary adaptation through lineage-based pitch inheritance (Experiment~4).

\section{The Psychoacoustic Landscape}

Unlike the tunably rugged landscapes common in artificial life \citep{wright1932,kauffman1993}, Conchordal's landscape is derived deterministically from physical acoustics and cochlear mechanics, not from arbitrary fitness functions.

\subsection{Log-Frequency Representation}

All spectral relations are computed in $x = \log_2(f)$ space, where frequency ratios become additive displacements ($\Delta x = \log_2(f_2/f_1)$). This translational invariance lets harmonic templates and interference kernels be defined as convolutions, and is essential for modelling perceptual similarity structured by integer ratios \citep{glasberg1990}.

\subsection{Harmonicity}

Harmonicity $H(x)$ quantifies how well spectral energy at position $x$ in log$_2$-frequency space is explained by a harmonic template \citep{terhardt1979,parncutt1988}. Periodicity extraction---the neural basis of virtual pitch---is well localized in the auditory brainstem and primary auditory cortex, making $H$ a relatively low-level quantity (though its perceptual weighting relative to roughness varies across cultures; see Limitations).

Computation proceeds by sibling projection, a two-pass convolution with power-law weights $w_k=k^{-\rho}$ ($\rho>0$):
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Roots}(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} S(x+\log_2 k)\;k^{-\rho},
      \label{eq:h-down}\\
  H(x) &= \sum_{m=1}^{N} \mathrm{Roots}(x-\log_2 m)\;m^{-\rho}.
      \label{eq:h-up}
\end{align}
%
The downward pass (\ref{eq:h-down}) accumulates evidence for virtual roots; the upward pass (\ref{eq:h-up}) redistributes that evidence to harmonics. For two tones at frequency ratio $p\!:\!q$, coincident harmonics accumulate constructively; the number of such coincidences within order $N$ grows as $\max(p,q)$ shrinks, so peaks at simpler integer ratios are systematically stronger (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}A).

In all experiments $N=16$ and $\rho=0.4$. Overtone and undertone projections are blended with equal weight. The field is normalized to $H_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Roughness}

Roughness $R(x)$ quantifies sensory dissonance from unresolved beating within the critical band \citep{plomp1965}. Like harmonicity, roughness reflects early peripheral processing: it arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane, a mechanism rooted in cochlear mechanics \citep{glasberg1990}.

The computation maps the amplitude spectrum $S$ onto the ERB-rate scale $z = 21.4\log_{10}(0.00437f+1)$ and convolves with a Plomp--Levelt interference kernel:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\mathrm{raw}}(z) = \int S(\zeta)\;g_{\mathrm{PL}}(|z-\zeta|)
      \,d\zeta,
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
%
where $g_{\mathrm{PL}}$ peaks at approximately $0.25$\,ERB and vanishes at both zero separation (no self-interference) and wide separation (outside the critical band). At simple integer ratios $p\!:\!q$, harmonics are spaced well beyond the critical band, producing roughness troughs that complement the harmonicity peaks (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}B).

The raw output is normalized via a reference-based saturation map to $R_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Consonance}

Unlike harmonicity and roughness, consonance is not a single well-defined perceptual quantity; it recruits higher-order circuits and is shaped by enculturation \citep{mcdermott2016}. No unique mapping $C(H,R)$ can be stipulated on purely psychoacoustic grounds. Because Conchordal uses consonance for landscape evaluation, metabolic signaling, and proposal weighting, we adopt a bilinear consonance core---the most general bilinear form for two $[0,1]$ inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
C = a\,H_{01} + b\,R_{01} + c\,H_{01}\,R_{01} + d.
\label{eq:consonance-core}
\end{equation}
%
Here $b<0$ penalizes roughness, while the interaction term $c$ modulates the roughness penalty conditionally on harmonicity. Different coefficient settings yield task-specific quantities from the same functional family.

The evaluation field for local pitch adaptation uses $(a,b,c,d)=(1,-1.35,1,0)$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{field}} = H_{01} - 1.35\,R_{01} + H_{01}\,R_{01}.
\end{equation}

A second parameterisation, $C_{\text{density}} = H_{01}(1 - R_{01})$ (setting $b\!=\!0,\,c\!=\!-1$), provides a nonnegative mass used in Conchordal's interactive mode for spawn-position weighting; it is not used in the experiments reported here but is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}D for comparison. Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}C,D illustrate the resulting landscape for a single-anchor scan; both views exhibit attractor structure near simple integer ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3, 5:4), consistent with classical observations on consonance \citep{helmholtz1877,sethares1993}. No discrete scale was imposed; the attractors emerge solely from psychoacoustic structure.

For bounded metabolic control, $C_{\text{field}}$ is passed through a sigmoid: $C_{\text{level01}} = \sigma\!\bigl(\beta(C_{\text{field}}-\theta)\bigr)\in[0,1]$, with $(\beta,\theta)=(2,0)$.

\section{Agents and Dynamics}

\subsection{Pitch Adaptation}
\label{sec:pitch-adapt}

Each agent proposes local pitch perturbations of magnitude $\lambda=0.15$\,st sampled within a bounded neighborhood in log-frequency space. A hill-climbing acceptance rule evaluates candidate positions via $C_{\text{field}}$ and a density-based repulsion term. Repulsion is computed through a Gaussian kernel ($\sigma=0.06$\,st) over pitch distances, discouraging collapse to identical frequencies. Proposals are accepted with probability $\min\!\bigl(1,\,\exp\bigl((\Delta S)/T\bigr)\bigr)$ where $\Delta S = S_{\text{new}} - S_{\text{old}}$ is the change in the combined score ($C_{\text{field}}$ minus repulsion) and $T$ decays exponentially ($T_0=0.05$, $\tau=30$\,steps), annealing toward greedy hill-climbing.

Ablation conditions remove either the hill-climbing mechanism or the repulsion term.

\subsection{Metabolic Policy}

Agents possess energy $E$. Dynamics follow:

\begin{equation}
E' = E - c_b \Delta t - c_a I_{\text{attack}} + r_E \cdot C_{\text{level01}} \cdot I_{\text{attack}},
\end{equation}

where $c_b$ is basal cost, $c_a$ attack cost, $r_E$ recharge coefficient, and $I_{\text{attack}}\in\{0,1\}$ indicates a sound-onset event ($I_{\text{attack}}=1$ when the agent's phase oscillator crosses zero with $E>0$). Agents start with $E=1.0$ and perish when $E\leq 0$. We set $c_b=0.5$/s, $c_a=0.02$ per event, and $r_E=0.5$ per attack; time advances in hops of $512$ samples at $48\,$kHz ($\Delta t\approx 10.7\,$ms). In the baseline condition, recharge scales with consonance ($r_E=0.5$); in the ablation condition, $r_E=0$.

\subsection{Temporal Oscillation}

Each agent contains a phase oscillator $\dot{\phi_i} = \omega_i + (K/N)\sum_j \sin(\phi_j - \phi_i) + F(t)$, where $K$ is coupling strength and $F(t)$ is an optional external forcing term (``kick''). Phase locking is quantified via the Kuramoto order parameter $R(t)$ \citep{kuramoto1984,strogatz2000} and phase-locking value (PLV) \citep{lachaux1999}.

The Kuramoto model realizes the temporal axis of DCC: cortical oscillations entrain to periodic auditory stimuli \citep{lakatos2008,doelling2015}, and neural phase-locking predicts perceived rhythmic salience \citep{nozaradan2011}. Agent timing thus shares the coupling law hypothesized for cortical beat tracking \citep{large1999}.

\subsection{Self-Organized Polyphony}

Each run begins with a dissonance-seeking phase (rounds 1--50) followed by a consonance-seeking phase (rounds 51--100); the first 10 post-switch rounds are discarded as burn-in. The initial phase disperses agents, improving diversity; a consonance-only control (Supplementary S6) confirms that polyphony also emerges without this curriculum, though with fewer unique pitch positions. Three conditions were compared: baseline, no hill-climb, and no repulsion (Figures~\ref{fig:e2-dynamics}--\ref{fig:e2-intervals}).

\textbf{Diversity.} Unique pitch bins averaged $19.2 \pm 1.4$ (baseline) versus $13.1 \pm 1.3$ (no repulsion; Welch $t=14.5$, $p<10^{-13}$, Cohen's $d=4.6$). Mean nearest-neighbor distance was $0.57 \pm 0.05$\,st (baseline), dropping to $0.29 \pm 0.11$\,st under no-repulsion ($t=11.0$, $p<10^{-11}$, $d=3.5$), confirming pitch collapse.

\textbf{Interval Structure.} Shannon entropy of the pairwise interval distribution (240 bins of 0.05\,st over 0--12\,st; uniform maximum $\ln 240 = 5.48$\,nats) was $3.95 \pm 0.17$ nats (baseline), rising to $4.92 \pm 0.06$ under no-hill-climbing ($t=-23.4$, $p<10^{-22}$, $d=7.4$) and falling to $3.57 \pm 0.29$ under no-repulsion ($t=5.1$, $p<10^{-4}$, $d=1.6$). The no-hill increase toward the uniform ceiling reflects a distribution lacking consonant peaks; the no-repulsion decrease reflects collapse toward fewer intervals.

These results show that hill-climbing enriches consonant interval structure, while repulsion prevents monophonic collapse. Self-organized harmonic distributions arise from the landscape--agent interaction. A shuffled-landscape control (Discussion, \S Terrain Validity) further shows that this structure requires coherent psychoacoustic gradients: destroying spatial coherence while preserving the marginal score distribution raises interval entropy from $3.93$ to $4.65$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$).

\subsection{Consonance as Selection Pressure}

Each run maintained agents within $\pm 1$ octave of the 220\,Hz anchor; dead agents were immediately respawned at random positions until 200 deaths accumulated ($\approx$4{,}000 pooled lifetimes per condition across 20 seeds). Early consonance $C_{\mathrm{firstK}}$ (mean $C_{\text{level01}}$ over the first $K\!=\!20$ ticks) strongly predicted lifetime under baseline recharge (Figure~\ref{fig:e3-selection}). Seed-level analysis ($n\!=\!20$, Fisher $z$) yielded mean Pearson $r = 0.73$ (95\% CI $[0.69, 0.76]$; one-sample $t(19) = 24.6$, $p < 0.001$; range $0.56$--$0.86$). Pooled ($n\!\approx\!4{,}000$): $r = 0.73$, Spearman $\rho_s = 0.45$, log-rank $p < 0.001$; median survival 371 vs.\ 339.

When recharge was disabled, seed-level mean $r = 0.003 \pm 0.08$, not significantly different from zero; the condition contrast was significant (Welch $t(27.0) = 22.2$, $p < 0.001$, Fisher $z$). Because pitch adaptation is disabled in this experiment (Table~\ref{tab:conditions}), agents do not move, so early consonance is determined by initial pitch position; the recharge ablation confirms that the survival differential requires consonance-dependent energy replenishment, not merely positional coincidence. The survival advantage is an expected consequence of the consonance-dependent metabolic design; this experiment validates that the mechanism operates quantitatively as intended and that its removal cleanly eliminates the effect. Consonance thus functions as an ecological resource, generating selection pressure through the metabolic pathway.

\subsection{Consonance-Gated Entrainment}

In Conchordal, consonance modulates metabolic energy, which determines vitality, which can scale the strength of Kuramoto phase coupling. This chain predicts---by design---that agents at consonant pitch positions should synchronize more readily to an external rhythm than those at dissonant positions. The following controlled assay validates that this consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway operates as intended and isolates it from confounding dynamics.

Agent pitches are assigned by stratified consonance sampling within $\pm 1$ octave of the anchor (one pitch per equal-width consonance stratum). To isolate the consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway, we use a simplified continuous energy model (omitting the attack-gated dynamics of \S3.2): $dE/dt = -c_b + r_E \cdot C_i$ ($c_b\!=\!0.05$/s, $r_E\!=\!0.1$/s---note the continuous-time units, distinct from the per-attack $r_E$ in \S3.2---, $E_{\mathrm{cap}}\!=\!1.0$). Vitality $v = \sqrt{E / E_{\mathrm{cap}}}$ scales coupling to a 2\,Hz external kick ($K_{\mathrm{base}}\!=\!3.0$; intrinsic frequency $1.8\!\cdot\!2\pi$\,rad/s, $\pm 2\%$ jitter). Three conditions: \emph{vitality} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot v_i$), \emph{uniform} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot \bar{v}$), and \emph{control} ($k_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$); 40\,s runs, 20 seeds each.

Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}A shows the group mean PLV over time. Both vitality and uniform conditions synchronize above the control baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}B plots per-agent final PLV against consonance (using stratified pitch assignment to span the full consonance range): in the vitality condition, consonant agents achieve near-unity PLV while dissonant agents remain desynchronized (the dashed curve is a logistic sigmoid fit, grid-search OLS), whereas the control condition shows no such gradient. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}C summarizes the Pearson $r$(consonance, PLV) across all seeds. The vitality condition yields a strong positive correlation ($r = 0.75 \pm 0.09$), confirming that consonance position selectively modulates synchronization strength. The uniform condition, despite identical total coupling, shows near-zero correlation ($r \approx 0.0$), isolating the effect to per-agent vitality modulation rather than aggregate coupling magnitude. This experiment is a pathway-isolation assay: it validates one designed spectral-to-temporal bridge, not that rhythmic organization in the full runtime is determined by consonance alone.

\subsection{Hereditary Adaptation}
\label{sec:e6}

Experiments 1--3 establish selection but not heredity. To test whether lineage-based inheritance can consolidate ecological advantage, we compare two respawn conditions: \emph{heredity}, where a dead agent's replacement pitch is drawn $\mathcal{N}(\text{random surviving parent},\,\sigma\!=\!0.003\,\text{log}_2)$, versus \emph{random} uniform respawn within $\pm 1$ octave. Both conditions retain repulsion but disable hill-climbing, isolating hereditary selection as the sole consonance-improvement pathway (remaining parameters in Table~\ref{tab:conditions}). Twenty seeds per condition were run until 4{,}000 deaths ($\approx 125$ turnover cycles).

Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary} shows that heredity runs produce a statistically significant increase in $C_{\text{score}}$ relative to random controls. Welch's $t$-test confirmed higher consonance change in the heredity condition ($\Delta C_{\text{score}} = +0.121 \pm 0.177$ vs.\ $-0.003 \pm 0.027$; $t=3.10$, $p=0.001$, Cohen's $d=0.98$). Final consonance was likewise higher (heredity $0.185 \pm 0.178$ vs.\ random $0.061 \pm 0.018$; $t=3.13$, $p<0.001$). Pitch entropy dropped sharply under heredity ($1.67 \pm 0.46$ vs.\ $3.78 \pm 0.03$), indicating convergence toward specific pitch regions. The large inter-seed variance ($\pm 0.177$) reflects drift-dominated early dynamics: 8 of 20 heredity seeds showed $\Delta C > 0.1$ while 7 remained near baseline, consistent with stochastic founder effects \citep{kimura1983}. The final pitch histogram (Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}C) shows that heredity runs concentrate near consonant ratios rather than remaining uniformly dispersed. This convergence arises purely from differential survival and inheritance: without hill-climbing, only the metabolic selection pressure identified in Experiment~2 drives the effect. Parameters were selected from a sweep over 15 conditions (Supplementary Table~S2): the heredity--random separation was positive in all 15 and significant ($p<0.05$) in 11; the reported setting ($\sigma\!=\!0.003$, 4{,}000 deaths, $N\!=\!32$) is representative rather than the single best arm. To verify generalisation, we re-scored final configurations with an independent metric $\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}} = \binom{N}{2}^{-1}\!\sum_{i<j} \max_{p:q}\!\bigl[\tfrac{1}{pq}\,e^{-\alpha\,(\Delta_{ij}-r_{pq})^2}\bigr]$, the mean pairwise Gaussian proximity ($\alpha\!=\!4.0$\,st$^{-2}$) to just-intonation ratios $p\!:\!q$ ($p,q \leq 8$), weighted by complexity $1/(pq)$: heredity scored substantially higher ($0.55 \pm 0.13$ vs.\ $0.11 \pm 0.01$; Welch $t=15.3$, $p<10^{-12}$, $d=4.8$).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Integrated Interpretation}

The four experiments form a cumulative argument. Self-organized polyphony (Experiment~1) shows that the landscape admits structured configurations beyond trivial collapse; metabolic selection (Experiment~2) validates that consonance-dependent recharge produces quantifiable survival differentials; entrainment gating (Experiment~3) validates the consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway linking the spectral and temporal axes; and hereditary adaptation (Experiment~4) shows that selection pressure, once established, can accumulate across generations via inheritance alone. Each experiment isolates one mechanism; together they show that a psychoacoustic landscape can sustain the core ingredients of an artificial ecology without symbolic encoding. As a preliminary integration test, we ran the Experiment~4 protocol under four conditions crossing heredity/random respawn with hill-climbing on/off (20 seeds each). Figure~\ref{fig:e6-integration} shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields $C_{\text{score}} = 0.53$, exceeding heredity-only ($0.19$), hill-only ($0.11$), and random ($0.06$). Because all four conditions share the same 20 random seeds, the interaction contrast is computed per-seed (HH$_i$ $-$ H$_i$ $-$ RH$_i$ $+$ R$_i$; mean $0.30$, 95\% CI $[0.20, 0.39]$; $p < 0.001$, sign-flip permutation), confirming that the combination exceeds the sum of individual effects. All conditions terminate at 4{,}000 deaths; step count provides finer temporal resolution for the trajectory. A systematic factorial study is left to future work.

\subsection{The Ecological--Aesthetic Duality}

Because the fitness landscape is derived from perceptual consonance, ecological viability and musical coherence are, by design, the same quantity---a structural consequence of DCC. Its nontrivial content lies in falsifiable predictions: listeners should rate emergent configurations as more consonant than ablated controls, and cortical entrainment to DCC-generated rhythms should exceed non-DCC baselines (see Limitations). Whether the gap closes for human listeners remains an open empirical question. A potential objection is that unison is ecologically optimal yet aesthetically trivial; repulsion breaks this degeneracy (Experiment~1).

\subsection{Terrain Validity}

To test whether the observed polyphony depends specifically on coherent psychoacoustic terrain structure rather than on an arbitrary scalar fitness field, we introduced matched terrain controls (Supplementary~S7).
In a shuffled-landscape control, a fixed random permutation of the consonance-score bins was applied at each sweep, preserving the marginal score distribution while destroying spatial coherence.
Pitch spread remained intact ($19.2$ vs.\ $19.5$ unique bins, $p = 0.54$), indicating that repulsion alone prevents collapse, but interval structure degraded substantially: entropy rose from $3.93 \pm 0.16$ to $4.65 \pm 0.10$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$), approaching the uniform distribution.
Thus, it is not merely the presence of a fitness signal that matters; a coherent psychoacoustic gradient is required for the emergence of structured polyphony.
A coefficient sweep over 16 $(b,c)$ kernel settings further confirmed that polyphonic diversity is robust: unique pitch-bin counts remain $\geq 19$ across the grid (Supplementary Table~6).
By contrast, moderate harmonicity--roughness misalignment did not significantly disrupt polyphony metrics at the present resolution, suggesting that coarse ecological organisation depends more on coherent gradient topology than on exact H--R registration.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}

All in-simulation metrics ($C_{\text{score}}$, $C_{\mathrm{firstK}}$, PLV) are derived from the same psychoacoustic model that defines the fitness landscape; the terrain controls above address the resulting circularity for structural claims, but behavioural listening tests remain necessary to evaluate whether emergent configurations also predict human aesthetic judgements. The independent ratio-complexity check partially mitigates metric circularity: it returned uniformly low $\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}}$ in Experiment~1 ($\approx 0.11$), showing that the metric does not echo the training objective, while confirming the large heredity--random differentiation in Experiment~4. Because harmonicity--roughness weighting varies across listeners and cultures \citep{mcdermott2016}, the present coefficients implicitly encode a Western-leaning profile; listener-informed reparametrisation could enable non-Western perceptual weightings.

Experiment~4's hereditary adaptation converges to fixed-landscape attractors and does not constitute open-ended evolution \citep{bedau2000}; whether evolvable timbres or coupling topologies could support open-ended dynamics remains open. The consonance landscape currently assumes harmonic spectra; extending to inharmonic or noise-bearing timbres would test whether DCC generalizes beyond tonal contexts. More broadly, closing the loop via real-time listener biosignals would turn perception and generation into a coupled dynamical system.

\section{Conclusion}

Conchordal presents a psychoacoustic artificial life environment whose design is grounded in Direct Cognitive Coupling. Harmonicity and roughness define a continuous landscape that supports self-organization, selection, consonance-gated entrainment, and hereditary adaptation---core ingredients of artificial ecology instantiated in an auditory medium structured by perceptual law, complementing the spatial and chemical substrates traditionally studied in ALife. As an instrument, Conchordal offers composers a generative substrate in which ecological dynamics produce musically coherent material without symbolic specification, though formal perceptual evaluation is deferred to future work. More broadly, DCC is not inherently auditory: any creative medium whose perceptual basis admits quantitative modelling could host an analogous ecology.
