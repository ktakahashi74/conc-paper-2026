\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Conchordal}, a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition whose sonic agents are governed by artificial life dynamics within a psychoacoustic fitness landscape defined in log-frequency space. The system is built on the principle of \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC): the generative process operates directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries. The environment integrates roughness and harmonicity into a continuous consonance field without presupposing musical scales or harmonic rules. Agents adjust pitch through local proposal-and-accept dynamics under density-based repulsion, regulate survival via a consonance-dependent metabolic policy, and entrain temporally through Kuramoto-style phase coupling grounded in neural oscillatory models. Four experiments are reported. (1) Populations self-organize into structured, non-collapsed polyphony with enriched consonant interval distributions. (2) When consonance modulates metabolic recharge, early consonance strongly predicts lifetime, whereas the effect vanishes without recharge. (3) In a controlled entrainment assay, consonance-dependent vitality biases synchronization: agents at consonant positions synchronize more readily. (4) Hereditary pitch inheritance drives polyphonic convergence to multiple consonant ratios through differential survival alone. These findings demonstrate that consonance can function as an ecological resource in a non-traditional computational medium, yielding self-organization, selection, synchronization, and hereditary adaptation. Because DCC grounds the fitness landscape in perceptual structure, an ecological--aesthetic duality emerges: adaptive success and musical coherence become structurally aligned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Artificial life has traditionally unfolded in spatial, chemical, or symbolic domains \citep{langton1989,bedau2000}. Yet sound—despite its rich physical structure and deep entanglement with biological perception—remains underexplored as a primary computational medium for life-like dynamics. Conchordal is a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition that treats consonance as an ecological resource within a continuous psychoacoustic field inhabited by autonomous agents.

Conchordal is built on the principle of \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC): the generative process operates directly upon structures isomorphic to those in human perceptual cognition, bypassing symbolic intermediaries \citep{conchordal2025}. DCC goes beyond merely using a perceptually motivated fitness function: it requires that the generative dynamics themselves---agent movement rules, coupling laws, metabolic signals---be grounded in the same computational models that describe perception, so that the agent's ``world'' and the listener's perceptual space share a common structure. In the auditory case this means dispensing with scales, metrical grids, and note durations.

In the spectral domain, the fitness landscape is constructed from roughness and harmonicity models grounded in cochlear filtering and periodicity detection \citep{plomp1965,terhardt1979}. In the temporal domain, agent phase oscillators follow the same Kuramoto coupling law invoked by Dynamic Attending Theory to model cortical entrainment to rhythmic stimuli \citep{large1999,lakatos2008}. Because agents and listeners share the same perceptual geometry, the structures agents discover—consonant pitch configurations and phase-locked rhythms—are structurally aligned with perceptual salience, a prediction testable through listener experiments (\S6).

Rather than encoding harmony or rhythm as compositional rules, Conchordal defines a continuous field derived from this perceptual foundation. Agents do not “compose”; they seek locally advantageous regions of a cognitive landscape shaped by harmonicity and roughness. Consonance, in this system, is ecological before it is aesthetic. When linked to metabolism, it becomes a resource. When coupled to temporal oscillators, it enables entrainment.

\subsection*{Related Work}

\textbf{Sonic artificial life.}
Several systems have explored agent-based music generation \citep{miranda2002}. \citet{blackwell2002} used particle swarm optimization for real-time improvisation, with agents navigating a feature space shaped by performer feedback rather than perceptual models. \citet{mccormack2003} constructed evolutionary ecosystems in which agents reproduce and mutate sonic material. \citet{eldridge2009} recycled spectral energy through a filter-based ecosystem. \citet{eigenfeldt2013} deployed rule-based autonomous agents for real-time composition. \citet{bernardes2016} navigated a perceptually motivated tonal interval space for harmony generation. These systems operate in symbolic or feature-engineered spaces; Conchordal differs in grounding the landscape directly in psychoacoustic structure via DCC.

\textbf{Computational consonance.}
Beyond the classical Plomp--Levelt roughness model \citep{plomp1965} and Terhardt's virtual-pitch framework \citep{terhardt1979}, recent work has advanced periodicity-based consonance measures \citep{stolzenburg2015} and data-driven approaches using deep neural networks \citep{harrison2020}. Conchordal adopts a first-principles approach: roughness and harmonicity are computed from cochlear and periodicity models and combined through a bilinear consonance core, keeping the landscape interpretable and free of trained parameters.

\textbf{Embodied and predictive cognition.}
DCC resonates with embodied music cognition \citep{leman2007}, which argues that musical meaning is grounded in bodily and sensorimotor interaction with sound. It also connects to predictive processing frameworks \citep{clark2013}, in which perception and action share a common generative model. DCC operationalises a similar principle: agents and listeners share the same perceptual structure, so emergent configurations are salient without requiring a separate evaluative step.

The present study evaluates whether such a system exhibits core artificial life properties. The psychoacoustic landscape and its attractor structure are established in \S2. Four experiments then test self-organization under environmental constraints (Experiment~1), selection mediated by resource dynamics (Experiment~2), synchronization through external forcing (Experiment~3), and hereditary adaptation through lineage-based pitch inheritance (Experiment~4).

\section{The Psychoacoustic Landscape}

Unlike the tunably rugged landscapes common in artificial life \citep{wright1932,kauffman1993}, Conchordal's landscape is derived deterministically from physical acoustics and cochlear mechanics, not from arbitrary fitness functions. This grounding is what makes DCC possible.

\subsection{Log-Frequency Representation}

All spectral relations are computed in log$_2$ frequency space. This coordinate system renders frequency ratios translationally invariant: intervals correspond to additive displacements. Such invariance is essential for modeling perceptual similarity structured by integer ratios rather than absolute frequency \citep{glasberg1990}.

Let $x = \log_2(f)$. Intervals become $\Delta x = \log_2(f_2/f_1)$. This representation allows harmonic templates and interference kernels to be defined as convolutional operations across a continuous domain.

\subsection{Harmonicity}

Harmonicity $H(x)$ quantifies how well spectral energy at position $x$ in log$_2$-frequency space is explained by a harmonic template \citep{terhardt1979,parncutt1988}. Periodicity extraction---the neural basis of virtual pitch---is well localized in the auditory brainstem and primary auditory cortex, making $H$ a relatively low-level, culture-invariant quantity.

Computation proceeds by sibling projection, a two-pass convolution with power-law weights $w_k=k^{-\rho}$ ($\rho>0$):
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Roots}(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} S(x+\log_2 k)\;k^{-\rho},
      \label{eq:h-down}\\
  H(x) &= \sum_{m=1}^{N} \mathrm{Roots}(x-\log_2 m)\;m^{-\rho}.
      \label{eq:h-up}
\end{align}
%
The downward pass (\ref{eq:h-down}) accumulates evidence for virtual roots; the upward pass (\ref{eq:h-up}) redistributes that evidence to harmonics. For two tones at frequency ratio $p\!:\!q$, coincident harmonics accumulate constructively; the number of such coincidences within order $N$ grows as $\max(p,q)$ shrinks, so peaks at simpler integer ratios are systematically stronger (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}A).

In all experiments $N=16$ and $\rho=0.4$. Overtone and undertone projections are blended with equal weight; full parameterization is given in \citet{conchordal2025}. The field is normalized to $H_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Roughness}

Roughness $R(x)$ quantifies sensory dissonance from unresolved beating within the critical band \citep{plomp1965}. Like harmonicity, roughness reflects early peripheral processing: it arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane, a mechanism rooted in cochlear mechanics \citep{glasberg1990}.

The computation maps the amplitude spectrum $S$ onto the ERB-rate scale $z = 21.4\log_{10}(0.00437f+1)$ and convolves with a Plomp--Levelt interference kernel:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\mathrm{raw}}(z) = \int S(\zeta)\;g_{\mathrm{PL}}(|z-\zeta|)
      \,d\zeta,
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
%
where $g_{\mathrm{PL}}$ peaks at approximately $0.25$\,ERB and vanishes at both zero separation (no self-interference) and wide separation (outside the critical band). At simple integer ratios $p\!:\!q$, harmonics are spaced well beyond the critical band, producing roughness troughs that complement the harmonicity peaks (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}B).

The raw output is normalized via a reference-based saturation map to $R_{01}\in[0,1]$; details are given in \citet{conchordal2025}.

\subsection{Consonance}

Unlike harmonicity and roughness, which each correspond to a well-localized stage of auditory processing, consonance is not a single well-defined perceptual quantity. Its evaluation recruits higher-order circuits---from cortical pitch maps through prefrontal valuation networks---and is further shaped by enculturation and listening context. No unique mapping $C(H,R)$ can therefore be stipulated on purely psychoacoustic grounds.

In Conchordal, consonance serves multiple computational roles: landscape evaluation for hill-climbing pitch adaptation, bounded metabolic signaling, and nonnegative mass for density-weighted proposal sampling. We address both the definitional indeterminacy and this diversity of downstream uses by adopting a bilinear consonance core---the most general first-order interaction model for two $[0,1]$ inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
C = a\,H_{01} + b\,R_{01} + c\,H_{01}\,R_{01} + d.
\label{eq:consonance-core}
\end{equation}
%
Here $b<0$ penalizes roughness, while $c>0$ weakens that penalty when harmonicity is high. Different coefficient settings yield task-specific quantities from the same functional family.

The evaluation field for local pitch adaptation uses $(a,b,c,d)=(1,-1.35,1,0)$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{field}} = H_{01} - 1.35\,R_{01} + H_{01}\,R_{01}.
\end{equation}

For density-based proposal weighting, we set $(a,b,c,d)=(1,0,-\rho,0)$ with $\rho=1$, yielding a dedicated nonnegative mass:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{density}} = H_{01}(1 - R_{01}).
\end{equation}

This separation avoids conflating acceptance utility ($C_{\text{field}}$) with proposal density ($C_{\text{density}}$). Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}C,D illustrates the resulting landscape for a single-anchor scan; both views exhibit attractor structure near simple integer ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3, 5:4), consistent with classical observations on consonance \citep{helmholtz1877,sethares1993}. No discrete scale was imposed; the attractors emerge solely from psychoacoustic structure.

For bounded metabolic control, $C_{\text{field}}$ is passed through a sigmoid:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{level01}} = \sigma\!\bigl(\beta(C_{\text{field}}-\theta)\bigr)\in[0,1], \quad (\beta,\theta)=(2,0).
\end{equation}

\section{Agents and Dynamics}

\subsection{Pitch Adaptation}
\label{sec:pitch-adapt}

Each agent proposes local pitch perturbations of magnitude $\lambda=0.15$\,st sampled within a bounded neighborhood in log-frequency space. A hill-climbing acceptance rule evaluates candidate positions via $C_{\text{field}}$ and a density-based repulsion term. Repulsion is computed through a Gaussian kernel ($\sigma=0.06$\,st) over pitch distances, discouraging collapse to identical frequencies. Proposals are accepted stochastically with a temperature that decays exponentially ($T_0=0.05$, $\tau=30$\,steps), annealing toward greedy hill-climbing.

Ablation conditions remove either the hill-climbing mechanism or the repulsion term.

\subsection{Metabolic Policy}

Agents possess energy $E$. Dynamics follow:

\begin{equation}
E' = E - c_b \Delta t - c_a I_{\text{attack}} + r \cdot C_{\text{level01}} \cdot I_{\text{attack}},
\end{equation}

where $c_b$ is basal cost, $c_a$ attack cost, and $r$ recharge coefficient. Agents start with $E=1.0$ and perish when $E\leq 0$. The basal cost is $c_b=0.5$ energy per second, and time advances in hops of $512$ samples at $48\,$kHz ($\Delta t\approx 10.7\,$ms). In the baseline condition, recharge scales with consonance ($r>0$); in the ablation condition, $r=0$.

\subsection{Temporal Oscillation}

Each agent contains a phase oscillator:

\begin{equation}
\dot{\phi_i} = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N}\sum_j \sin(\phi_j - \phi_i) + F(t).
\end{equation}

Here $K$ is coupling strength and $F(t)$ is an optional external forcing term (“kick”). Phase locking is quantified via the Kuramoto order parameter $r(t)$ \citep{kuramoto1984,strogatz2000} and phase-locking value (PLV) \citep{lachaux1999}.

The Kuramoto model is motivated by neural oscillatory entrainment in auditory cognition. Cortical rhythms in the theta ($\sim$4--8\,Hz), alpha ($\sim$8--13\,Hz), and beta ($\sim$13--30\,Hz) bands entrain to periodic auditory stimuli \citep{lakatos2008,doelling2015}, and listeners' neural phase-locking to the beat predicts perceived rhythmic salience \citep{nozaradan2011}. This oscillator design thus realises the temporal axis of DCC: agent timing shares the coupling law hypothesised for cortical beat tracking \citep{large1999}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Self-Organized Polyphony}

Populations of 24 agents were simulated for 100 pitch-proposal rounds (burn-in 10; each round applies one proposal-and-accept cycle per agent) with the pitch adaptation parameters from the \emph{Pitch Adaptation} subsection above. Three conditions were compared: baseline, no hill-climb, and no repulsion.

\textbf{Diversity.} Unique pitch bins averaged $19.2 \pm 1.4$ (baseline) versus $13.1 \pm 1.3$ (no repulsion; Welch $t=14.5$, $p<10^{-13}$, Cohen's $d=4.6$). Mean nearest-neighbor distance was $0.57 \pm 0.05$\,st (baseline), dropping to $0.29 \pm 0.11$\,st under no-repulsion ($t=11.0$, $p<10^{-11}$, $d=3.5$), confirming pitch collapse.

\textbf{Interval Structure.} Shannon entropy of the pairwise interval distribution (240 bins of 0.05\,st over 0--12\,st; uniform maximum $\ln 240 = 5.48$\,nats) was $3.95 \pm 0.17$ nats (baseline), rising to $4.92 \pm 0.06$ under no-hill-climbing ($t=-23.4$, $p<10^{-22}$, $d=7.4$) and falling to $3.57 \pm 0.29$ under no-repulsion ($t=5.1$, $p<10^{-4}$, $d=1.6$). The no-hill increase toward the uniform ceiling reflects a distribution lacking consonant peaks; the no-repulsion decrease reflects collapse toward fewer intervals.

These results show that hill-climbing enriches consonant interval structure, while repulsion prevents monophonic collapse \citep{reynolds1987}. Self-organized harmonic distributions arise from the landscape--agent interaction.

\subsection{Consonance as Selection Pressure}

Each run placed 32 agents at random positions within $\pm 1$ octave of a 220\,Hz anchor (pitch locked at birth). Five seeds $\times$ two conditions yielded 1000 pooled agent lifetimes. Early consonance (first 20 ticks) strongly predicted lifetime under baseline recharge (Pearson $r = 0.71$; Spearman $\rho = 0.45$; log-rank $p < 0.001$). The high Pearson $r$ reflects the roughly linear, floor-bounded relationship visible in Figure~\ref{fig:e3-selection}C: agents below a consonance threshold die uniformly fast, while those above it survive in approximate proportion to consonance. Median survival differed markedly between high- and low-consonance groups (372 vs.\ 339). The effect was consistent at the seed level: all five seeds individually showed significant correlations (per-seed Pearson $r$ range $0.63$--$0.80$; all log-rank $p < 0.001$).

When recharge was disabled, correlations vanished (Pearson $r = 0.028$; log-rank $p = 0.33$; no individual seed reached $p < 0.05$). Thus, consonance functions as a genuine ecological resource, generating measurable selection pressure.

\subsection{Consonance-Gated Entrainment}

In Conchordal, consonance modulates metabolic energy, which determines vitality, which can scale the strength of Kuramoto phase coupling. This chain predicts that agents at consonant pitch positions should synchronize more readily to an external rhythm than those at dissonant positions---a \emph{consonance-gated} entrainment effect under controlled conditions.

We test this with 32 agents whose pitches are assigned by stratified sampling within $\pm 1$ octave of a 220\,Hz anchor: the landscape is scanned densely, raw consonance scores are normalised to $[0,1]$, and one pitch is drawn from each of 32 equal-width consonance strata so that the independent variable spans its full range. Energy evolves as $dE/dt = -c_b + r \cdot C_i$ (basal drain $c_b=0.05$/s, recharge rate $r=0.1$/s, capacity $E_{\mathrm{cap}}=1.0$). Vitality $v = \sqrt{E / E_{\mathrm{cap}}}$ then scales coupling to a 2\,Hz external kick ($K_{\mathrm{base}}=3.0$). Intrinsic frequencies are $1.8\cdot2\pi$\,rad/s with $\pm 2\%$ jitter. Three conditions are compared: \emph{vitality} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot v_i$), \emph{uniform} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot \bar{v}$, same total coupling budget distributed evenly), and \emph{control} ($k_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$). Each condition was run for 40\,s ($dt=0.02$\,s) across 20 seeds.

Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}A shows the group mean PLV over time. Both vitality and uniform conditions synchronize above the control baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}B plots per-agent final PLV against consonance (using stratified pitch assignment to span the full consonance range): in the vitality condition, consonant agents achieve near-unity PLV while dissonant agents remain desynchronized, whereas the control condition shows no such gradient. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}C summarizes the Pearson $r$(consonance, PLV) across all seeds. The vitality condition yields a strong positive correlation ($r = 0.75 \pm 0.09$), confirming that consonance position selectively modulates synchronization strength. The uniform condition, despite identical total coupling, shows near-zero correlation ($r \approx 0.0$), isolating the effect to per-agent vitality modulation rather than aggregate coupling magnitude.

\subsection{Hereditary Adaptation}

Experiments 1--3 establish selection but not heredity: agents survive differentially yet do not transmit traits to offspring. To test whether lineage-based inheritance can consolidate ecological advantage, we compare two respawn conditions. In the \emph{heredity} condition, a dead agent is replaced by an offspring whose pitch is drawn from a normal distribution centered on a randomly chosen surviving parent ($\sigma=0.003$\,log$_2$, i.e.\ $\approx 0.036$\,st). In the \emph{random} condition, offspring pitch is drawn uniformly within $\pm 1$ octave of the anchor. Both conditions use density-based perceptual repulsion (Experiment~1 established that repulsion is necessary for polyphonic diversity) but disable consonance-based hill-climbing (landscape weight $= 0$), so that the only mechanism for consonance improvement is hereditary selection through metabolic advantage. All other parameters match Experiment~2 (32 agents, 220\,Hz anchor, metabolic recharge). Twenty seeds per condition were run until 4{,}000 deaths accumulated ($\approx 125$ generations).

Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary} shows that heredity runs produce a statistically significant increase in mean consonance relative to random controls. Welch's $t$-test confirmed higher consonance change in the heredity condition ($\Delta C_{\text{score}} = +0.121 \pm 0.177$ vs.\ $-0.003 \pm 0.027$; $t=3.10$, $p=0.001$, Cohen's $d=0.98$). Final consonance was likewise higher (heredity $0.185 \pm 0.178$ vs.\ random $0.061 \pm 0.018$; $t=3.13$, $p<0.001$). Pitch entropy dropped sharply under heredity ($1.67 \pm 0.46$ vs.\ $3.78 \pm 0.03$), indicating convergence toward specific pitch regions. The large variance across heredity seeds ($\pm 0.177$) reflects the stochastic nature of the process: some runs converge rapidly to consonant attractors while others remain near baseline, consistent with drift-dominated early dynamics \citep{kimura1983}. The final pitch histogram (Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}C) shows that heredity runs concentrate near consonant ratios rather than remaining uniformly dispersed. This convergence arises purely from differential survival and inheritance: without hill-climbing, only the metabolic selection pressure identified in Experiment~2 drives the effect. Mutation magnitude ($\sigma=0.003\,\log_2$) and generation count (4{,}000 deaths) were selected via a parameter sweep over 15 conditions to maximise the heredity--random separation.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

Conchordal demonstrates that psychoacoustic structure can support artificial life phenomena without recourse to symbolic encoding or predefined tonal systems. The landscape attractors (\S2) arise from perceptual primitives \citep{helmholtz1877,plomp1965,terhardt1979}; populations self-organize into structured polyphony through local optimization and density constraints; and consonance-dependent metabolism generates ecological selection \citep{bedau2000}.

Consonance-gated entrainment (Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}) demonstrates that pitch ecology and rhythmic synchronization are not independent: agents at consonant positions maintain higher vitality and synchronize more readily. The vitality condition produces a positive consonance--PLV correlation absent in the uniform control, confirming that the effect arises from per-agent vitality modulation rather than aggregate coupling strength. We interpret this as a controlled mechanism result---one spectral-to-temporal pathway (consonance $\to$ energy $\to$ vitality $\to$ coupling $\to$ phase-locking)---not as a claim that rhythm in the full system is exclusively consonance-driven.

Hereditary pitch inheritance produces a statistically significant increase in consonance over random respawn (Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}; $p=0.001$, $d=0.98$). Because consonance-based hill-climbing is disabled, the only source of consonance gain is differential survival and inheritance. Over $\approx$125 generations the heredity condition shows a significant consonance increase despite high inter-seed variance, demonstrating that metabolic selection pressure can accumulate across generations through hereditary transmission.

Collectively, these results illustrate DCC operating across both spectral and temporal axes: landscape attractors and polyphony arise from cochlear and periodicity-detection primitives, while a controlled consonance-gated phase-locking pathway links the spectral fitness landscape to temporal synchronization.

\subsection{The Ecological--Aesthetic Duality}

A structural duality underlies these results. Because the fitness landscape is derived from perceptual consonance, the conditions for artificial life and the conditions for musically coherent sound are one and the same: agents that survive are those that occupy consonant regions; populations that self-organize produce harmonically structured polyphony; lineages that inherit consonant pitch positions persist across generations; oscillators that entrain generate perceptually salient rhythms. Conversely, musically coherent configurations—consonant pitch arrangements, phase-locked pulse—are precisely those that sustain life-like dynamics in this system.

This duality is a design consequence of DCC, not an empirical discovery; yet it is nontrivial because it generates falsifiable predictions. If the duality holds, listeners should rate DCC-generated configurations as more consonant than ablated controls, and cortical entrainment to DCC rhythms should exceed that to non-DCC baselines (\S6). In a system whose fitness landscape is arbitrary, ecological success and aesthetic coherence may coincide only by chance. DCC narrows that gap: because the ecological resource \emph{is} perceptual consonance, adaptive fitness and aesthetic quality are structurally aligned.

A potential objection is that maximal consonance (e.g.\ all agents at unison) is ecologically optimal yet aesthetically trivial. In Conchordal this degeneracy is broken by repulsion: Experiment~1 shows that density-based avoidance is necessary for polyphonic structure, and removing it collapses the population to a single pitch. The duality therefore holds not for consonance alone but for the full ecological dynamic---consonance \emph{plus} repulsion---which jointly produce harmonic diversity. Any system built on DCC inherits this property; the ecological--aesthetic duality is a general consequence of the coupling principle, not an artifact of the particular auditory implementation presented here.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The claim that DCC-generated configurations are ``perceptually salient by construction'' has not yet been validated with human listeners. The present evidence is structural: the landscape is derived from perceptual models, and agents converge to its attractors. Whether listeners actually perceive emergent configurations as more consonant or rhythmically coherent than matched controls is an empirical question that requires perceptual experiments. The DCC hypothesis yields concrete testable predictions: (a)~emergent pitch configurations should receive higher consonance ratings than those from ablated or randomised processes; (b)~cortical entrainment (measured via frequency-tagged EEG) to agent-generated rhythms should exceed that to non-DCC controls. Until such validation is performed, the ecological--aesthetic duality remains a structural prediction rather than a demonstrated perceptual fact.

The bilinear consonance core (\ref{eq:consonance-core}) introduces coefficients $(a,b,c,d)$ whose values were chosen to produce qualitatively reasonable landscapes but have not been subjected to systematic sensitivity analysis. Different coefficient settings would shift the relative depth of roughness troughs and harmonicity peaks, potentially altering attractor strength and the balance between selection pressure and landscape ruggedness. A parametric sweep across coefficient space, measuring its effect on Experiments 1--2 outcomes, would clarify how robust the reported phenomena are to this design choice.

The consonance landscape currently assumes harmonic spectra. Extending the roughness and harmonicity kernels to inharmonic or noise-bearing timbres would broaden the ecology to percussion, bells, and other non-pitched sound sources, testing whether DCC generalises beyond tonal contexts.

The present system realises the first stage of DCC: mapping from cognitive model to acoustic environment. A natural next stage is closing the loop by feeding back listener biosignals---EEG, heart rate, galvanic skin response---to the system in real time \citep{conchordal2025}. Such a closed loop would allow the landscape itself to reshape in response to the listener's internal state, turning perception and generation into a coupled dynamical system.

\section{Conclusion}

Conchordal establishes a psychoacoustic artificial life environment in which consonance acts as an ecological resource. In log-frequency space, harmonicity and roughness define a continuous landscape supporting attractors, population-level structure, survival differentials, consonance-gated entrainment, and hereditary adaptation. These results suggest that life-like adaptive dynamics need not be confined to spatial or chemical substrates. Sound itself—structured by perceptual law—can sustain artificial ecologies. As an instrument, Conchordal offers composers a generative substrate in which ecological dynamics produce musically coherent material without symbolic specification.

More broadly, DCC is not specific to the auditory domain: any creative medium whose perceptual basis is well characterised could, in principle, host an analogous ecology \citep{conchordal2025}. Conchordal serves as a proof of concept for the auditory case; extending DCC to other sensory modalities is a direction for future work.
