\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces \textit{Conchordal}, a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition whose sonic agents are governed by artificial life dynamics within a psychoacoustic fitness landscape. The system is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a design principle requiring that generative dynamics operate directly on structures isomorphic to human perceptual cognition. The environment integrates roughness and harmonicity into a continuous consonance field without presupposing scales or harmonic rules. Agents adjust pitch through local proposal-and-accept dynamics under a crowding penalty, regulate survival via consonance-dependent metabolism, and entrain temporally through Kuramoto-style phase coupling. Four experiments are reported: (1)~populations self-organize into structured polyphony with enriched consonant intervals; (2)~consonance-dependent metabolism yields survival differentials that vanish when recharge is disabled; (3)~consonance-dependent vitality biases rhythmic synchronization; (4)~hereditary pitch inheritance drives polyphonic convergence through differential survival alone. An integration test shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields a consonance gain exceeding either alone; the interaction vanishes on a shuffled landscape and generalizes across three landscape topologies. These findings show that a psychoacoustically derived landscape serves as an effective artificial-life terrain, yielding self-organization, selection, synchronization, and hereditary adaptation in a non-traditional computational medium.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Artificial life has traditionally unfolded in spatial, chemical, or symbolic domains \citep{langton1989,bedau2000}. Yet sound---despite its rich physical structure and deep entanglement with biological perception---remains underexplored as a primary computational medium for life-like dynamics. Conchordal is a bio-acoustic instrument for generative composition that treats consonance as an ecological resource within a continuous psychoacoustic field inhabited by autonomous agents.

Conchordal is built on \emph{Direct Cognitive Coupling} (DCC), a design principle for perceptually grounded generative systems \citep{conchordal2025}: generative dynamics should operate directly within a landscape derived from quantitative models of perceptual processing, without symbolic intermediaries. In Conchordal, cochlear roughness and periodicity-based harmonicity define a continuous consonance landscape; agents inhabit this perceptual geometry, and their ecological outcomes---survival, reproduction, synchronization---are determined by landscape position.

The present study evaluates whether such a system exhibits core artificial life properties. The psychoacoustic landscape and its attractor structure are established in \S2. Four experiments then test self-organization under environmental constraints (Experiment~1), selection mediated by resource dynamics (Experiment~2), synchronization through external forcing (Experiment~3), and hereditary adaptation through lineage-based pitch inheritance (Experiment~4).

\subsection*{Related Work}

\textbf{Sonic artificial life.}
Agent-based music generation spans swarm improvisation \citep{blackwell2002}, evolutionary sonic ecosystems \citep{mccormack2003}, spectral-energy recycling \citep{eldridge2009}, rule-based autonomous composition \citep{eigenfeldt2013}, and perceptually motivated harmony navigation \citep{bernardes2016}; see \citet{miranda2002} for a broader survey. These systems incorporate acoustic or perceptual features into agent design, but the generative substrate---the space agents inhabit and the fitness they optimize---is not derived from a quantitative perceptual model. DCC addresses this gap.

\textbf{Computational consonance.}
Beyond the classical Plomp--Levelt roughness model \citep{plomp1965} and Terhardt's virtual-pitch framework \citep{terhardt1979}, recent work has advanced periodicity-based consonance measures \citep{stolzenburg2015} and data-driven approaches using deep neural networks \citep{harrison2020}. Conchordal adopts a first-principles approach: roughness and harmonicity are computed from cochlear and periodicity models and combined through a bilinear consonance core, keeping the landscape interpretable and free of trained parameters.

\textbf{Embodied and predictive cognition.}
DCC resonates with embodied music cognition \citep{leman2007} and predictive processing \citep{clark2013}, both grounding perception and generation in shared structures. DCC operationalises this for instrument design: agents and listeners inhabit the same perceptual geometry.

\section{The Psychoacoustic Landscape}

Unlike the tunably rugged landscapes common in artificial life \citep{wright1932,kauffman1993}, Conchordal's landscape is derived deterministically from physical acoustics and cochlear mechanics, not from arbitrary fitness functions.

\subsection{Log-Frequency Representation}

All spectral relations are computed in $x = \log_2(f)$ space, where frequency ratios become additive displacements ($\Delta x = \log_2(f_2/f_1)$). This translational invariance lets harmonic templates and interference kernels be defined as convolutions, and is essential for modelling perceptual similarity structured by integer ratios \citep{glasberg1990}. For readability, intervals and distances are reported in cents (ct); $1200$\,ct $= 1$\,octave $= 1$ unit in $x$ space ($1$\,semitone $= 100$\,ct).

\subsection{Harmonicity}

Harmonicity $H(x)$ quantifies how well spectral energy at position $x$ in $\log_2$-frequency space is explained by a harmonic template \citep{terhardt1979,parncutt1988}. Periodicity extraction---the neural basis of virtual pitch---is well localized in the auditory brainstem and primary auditory cortex, making $H$ a relatively low-level quantity (though its perceptual weighting relative to roughness varies across cultures; see Limitations).

Computation proceeds by sibling projection, a two-pass convolution with power-law weights $w_k=k^{-\rho}$ ($\rho>0$):
%
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{Roots}(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} S(x+\log_2 k)\;k^{-\rho},
      \label{eq:h-down}\\
  H(x) &= \sum_{m=1}^{N} \mathrm{Roots}(x-\log_2 m)\;m^{-\rho}.
      \label{eq:h-up}
\end{align}
%
The downward pass (\ref{eq:h-down}) accumulates evidence for virtual roots; the upward pass (\ref{eq:h-up}) redistributes that evidence to harmonics. For two tones at frequency ratio $p\!:\!q$, coincident harmonics accumulate constructively; the number of such coincidences within order $N$ grows as $\max(p,q)$ shrinks, so peaks at simpler integer ratios are systematically stronger (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}A).

In all experiments $N=16$ and $\rho=0.4$. Overtone and undertone projections are blended with equal weight. The field is normalized to $H_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Roughness}

Roughness $R(x)$ quantifies sensory dissonance from unresolved beating within the critical band \citep{plomp1965}. Like harmonicity, roughness reflects early peripheral processing: it arises from amplitude-modulation products on the basilar membrane, a mechanism rooted in cochlear mechanics \citep{glasberg1990}.

The computation maps the amplitude spectrum $S$ onto the ERB-rate scale
%
\begin{equation}
  z = 21.4\,\log_{10}(0.00437\,f+1)
  \label{eq:erb}
\end{equation}
%
and convolves with a Plomp--Levelt interference kernel:
%
\begin{equation}
  R_{\mathrm{raw}}(z) = \int S(\zeta)\;g_{\mathrm{PL}}(|z-\zeta|)
      \,d\zeta,
  \label{eq:roughness}
\end{equation}
%
where $g_{\mathrm{PL}}$ peaks at approximately $0.25$\,ERB and vanishes at both zero separation (no self-interference) and wide separation (outside the critical band). At simple integer ratios $p\!:\!q$, harmonics are spaced well beyond the critical band, producing roughness troughs that complement the harmonicity peaks (Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}B).

The raw output is normalized via a reference-based saturation map to $R_{01}\in[0,1]$.

\subsection{Consonance}

Unlike harmonicity and roughness, consonance is not a single well-defined perceptual quantity; it recruits higher-order circuits and is shaped by enculturation \citep{mcdermott2016}. No unique mapping $C(H,R)$ can be stipulated on purely psychoacoustic grounds. Because Conchordal uses consonance for landscape evaluation, metabolic signaling, and proposal weighting, we adopt a bilinear consonance core---the most general bilinear form for two $[0,1]$ inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
C = a\,H_{01} + b\,R_{01} + c\,H_{01}\,R_{01} + d.
\label{eq:consonance-core}
\end{equation}
%
Here $b<0$ penalizes roughness, while the interaction term $c$ modulates the roughness penalty conditionally on harmonicity. Different coefficient settings yield task-specific quantities from the same functional family.

The evaluation field for local pitch adaptation uses $(a,b,c,d)=(1,-1.35,1,0)$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{field}} = H_{01} - 1.35\,R_{01} + H_{01}\,R_{01}.
\end{equation}

A second parameterisation (setting $b\!=\!0,\,c\!=\!-1$),
%
\begin{equation}
  C_{\text{density}} = H_{01}(1 - R_{01}),
  \label{eq:c-density}
\end{equation}
%
provides a nonnegative mass used in Conchordal's interactive mode for spawn-position weighting; it is not used in the experiments reported here but is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}D for comparison. Figure~\ref{fig:e1-landscape}C,D illustrate the resulting landscape for a single-anchor scan; both views exhibit attractor structure near simple integer ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3, 5:4), consistent with classical observations on consonance \citep{helmholtz1877,sethares1993}. No discrete scale was imposed; the attractors emerge solely from psychoacoustic structure.

For bounded metabolic control, $C_{\text{field}}$ is passed through a sigmoid: $C_{\text{level01}} = \sigma\!\bigl(\beta(C_{\text{field}}-\theta)\bigr)\in[0,1]$, with $(\beta,\theta)=(2,0)$.

\section{Agents and Dynamics}

The Conchordal instrument is designed around an extensible agent architecture that accommodates diverse behavioural strategies---including stochastic candidate sampling, movement costs, and exploration--persistence trade-offs---without altering the underlying landscape. The experiments reported here deliberately adopt minimal dynamics to isolate the landscape's contribution to self-organisation from strategy-specific effects; each mechanism described below is a simplification of the corresponding instrument subsystem.

\subsection{Pitch Adaptation}
\label{sec:pitch-adapt}

Each agent proposes local pitch perturbations of magnitude $\lambda=15$\,ct sampled within a bounded neighborhood in log-frequency space. A hill-climbing acceptance rule evaluates candidate positions via $C_{\text{field}}$ and a crowding penalty computed in ERB space. The crowding kernel $K_{\text{crowd}}(\Delta_{\text{erb}})$ is derived analytically as the complement of the roughness kernel: $K_{\text{crowd}}(d) = \max\!\bigl(0,\; 1 - R(d/\kappa) / R_{\text{peak}}\bigr)$, where $R$ is the full Plomp--Levelt roughness kernel and $\kappa$ is a reach factor ($\kappa=2.1$). This construction repels agents that are too close in pitch---preventing collapse to unison---while yielding to landscape-mediated repulsion at wider separations where roughness peaks. The combined score is $S = C_{\text{field}} - \lambda_C \sum_{j \neq i} K_{\text{crowd}}(\Delta_{ij})$. Proposals are accepted with probability $\min\!\bigl(1,\,\exp\bigl(\Delta S/T\bigr)\bigr)$ where $\Delta S = S_{\text{new}} - S_{\text{old}}$ and $T$ decays exponentially ($T_0=0.05$, $\tau=30$\,steps), annealing toward greedy hill-climbing.

\subsection{Metabolic Policy}

Agents possess energy $E$. Dynamics follow:

\begin{equation}
E \leftarrow E - c_b \Delta t - c_a I_{\text{attack}} + r_E \cdot C_{\text{level01}} \cdot I_{\text{attack}},
\end{equation}

where $c_b$ is basal cost, $c_a$ attack cost, $r_E$ recharge coefficient, and $I_{\text{attack}}\in\{0,1\}$ indicates a sound-onset event ($I_{\text{attack}}=1$ when the agent's phase oscillator crosses zero with $E>0$). Agents start with $E=1.0$ and perish when $E\leq 0$. We set $c_b=0.5$/s, $c_a=0.02$ per event, and $r_E=0.5$ per attack; time advances in hops of $512$ samples at $48\,$kHz ($\Delta t\approx 10.7\,$ms). In the baseline condition, recharge scales with consonance ($r_E=0.5$); in the ablation condition, $r_E=0$. An agent's \emph{vitality} $v = \sqrt{E/E_{\text{cap}}}$ (where $E_{\text{cap}}=1.0$) provides a normalised vigour signal available to downstream dynamics such as phase coupling.

\subsection{Temporal Oscillation}

Neural oscillations in auditory cortex entrain to periodic acoustic stimuli across multiple frequency bands \citep{lakatos2008,doelling2015}, and computational models of beat tracking posit coupled-oscillator dynamics as the underlying mechanism \citep{large1999}. Conchordal's full implementation realises this temporal axis of DCC through a multi-band modulation bank spanning delta through beta frequencies ($\approx$0.5--30\,Hz), mirroring layered temporal processing in cortical auditory networks. Agents couple to this shared oscillatory field rather than directly to one another (mean-field approximation), and the interaction is bidirectional: the field entrains agent timing while agent onsets excite the field.

The experiments extract the core coupling law as a single Kuramoto phase oscillator per agent:
%
\begin{equation}
\dot{\phi}_i = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N}\sum_j \sin(\phi_j - \phi_i) + F(t),
\end{equation}
%
where $K$ is coupling strength and $F(t)$ an external forcing term. Because metabolic vitality $v_i$ can gate the effective coupling ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot v_i$), the spectral landscape (consonance $\to$ energy $\to$ vitality) directly modulates temporal synchronisation---a bridge tested in Experiment~3. Phase locking is quantified via the Kuramoto order parameter $R(t)$ \citep{kuramoto1984,strogatz2000} and phase-locking value (PLV) \citep{lachaux1999}.

\subsection{Experiment~1: Self-Organized Polyphony}

Pitch adaptation is tested on a discrete log-frequency grid (0.005\,oct bins). Ablation conditions remove either the hill-climbing mechanism or the crowding penalty. Leave-one-out consonance is evaluated by removing each agent's spectral contribution from the shared environment before rescoring---an exact LOO that the real-time instrument approximates with a lighter-weight harmonic-projection subtraction. The leave-one-out mean of $C_{\text{field}}$ across all agents defines $C_{\text{score}}$, the primary outcome metric.

Each run begins with a dissonance-seeking phase (steps 1--50) followed by a consonance-seeking phase (steps 51--100); the initial dissonance-seeking phase disperses agents across the landscape, preventing premature collapse to a single attractor; the switch is implemented by negating the hill-climbing score ($S \to -S$), so agents descend the consonance landscape in the first phase and ascend it in the second. The first 10 post-switch steps are discarded as burn-in. The initial phase disperses agents, improving diversity; a consonance-only control (Supplementary S6) confirms that polyphony also emerges without this curriculum, though with fewer unique pitch positions. Three conditions were compared: baseline, no hill-climb, and no crowding (Figures~\ref{fig:e2-dynamics}--\ref{fig:e2-intervals}).

\textbf{Diversity.} Unique pitch bins averaged $19.5 \pm 1.6$ (baseline) versus $13.8 \pm 1.8$ (no crowding; Welch $t=10.6$, $p<10^{-12}$, Cohen's $d=3.3$). Mean nearest-neighbor distance was $62 \pm 9$\,ct (baseline), dropping to $34 \pm 9$\,ct under no-crowding ($t=9.8$, $p<10^{-11}$, $d=3.1$), confirming pitch collapse.

\textbf{Interval Structure.} Shannon entropy of the pairwise interval distribution (240 bins of 5\,ct over 0--1200\,ct; uniform maximum $\ln 240 = 5.48$\,nats) was $3.94 \pm 0.18$ nats (baseline), rising to $4.92 \pm 0.06$ under no-hill-climbing ($t=-23.2$, $p<10^{-17}$, $d=7.3$) and falling to $3.53 \pm 0.27$ under no-crowding ($t=5.7$, $p<10^{-5}$, $d=1.8$). The no-hill increase toward the uniform ceiling reflects a distribution lacking consonant peaks; the no-crowding decrease reflects collapse toward fewer intervals.

These results show that hill-climbing enriches consonant interval structure, while the crowding penalty prevents monophonic collapse. Self-organized harmonic distributions arise from the landscape--agent interaction. A shuffled-landscape control (Discussion, \S Terrain Validity) further shows that this structure requires coherent psychoacoustic gradients: destroying spatial coherence while preserving the marginal score distribution raises interval entropy from $3.91$ to $4.61$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$).

\subsection{Experiment~2: Consonance as Selection Pressure}

Each run maintained agents within $\pm 1$ octave of the 220\,Hz anchor; dead agents were immediately respawned at random positions until 200 deaths accumulated ($\approx$4{,}000 pooled lifetimes per condition across 20 seeds). Early consonance $C_{\mathrm{firstK}}$ (mean $C_{\text{level01}}$ over the first $K\!=\!20$ ticks) strongly predicted lifetime under baseline recharge (Figure~\ref{fig:e3-selection}). Seed-level analysis ($n\!=\!20$, Fisher $z$) yielded mean Pearson $r = 0.73$ (95\% CI $[0.69, 0.76]$; $p < 0.001$). Pooled ($n\!\approx\!4{,}000$): $r = 0.73$; median survival 371 vs.\ 339.

When recharge was disabled, seed-level mean $r = 0.003 \pm 0.08$, not significantly different from zero; the condition contrast was significant (Welch $t(27.0) = 22.2$, $p < 0.001$, Fisher $z$). Because pitch adaptation is disabled in this experiment (Table~\ref{tab:conditions}), agents do not move, so early consonance is determined by initial pitch position; the recharge ablation confirms that the survival differential requires consonance-dependent energy replenishment, not merely positional coincidence. The survival advantage is an expected consequence of the consonance-dependent metabolic design; this experiment validates that the mechanism operates quantitatively as intended and that its removal cleanly eliminates the effect. Consonance thus functions as an ecological resource, generating selection pressure through the metabolic pathway.

\subsection{Experiment~3: Consonance-Gated Entrainment}

In Conchordal, consonance modulates metabolic energy, which determines vitality, which can scale the strength of Kuramoto phase coupling. This chain predicts---by design---that agents at consonant pitch positions should synchronize more readily to an external rhythm than those at dissonant positions. The following controlled assay validates that this consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway operates as intended and isolates it from confounding dynamics.

Agent pitches are assigned by stratified consonance sampling within $\pm 1$ octave of the anchor (one pitch per equal-width consonance stratum). To isolate the consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway, we use a simplified continuous energy model (omitting the attack-gated dynamics of the Metabolic Policy section): $dE/dt = -c_b + r_E \cdot C_i$ ($c_b\!=\!0.05$/s, $r_E\!=\!0.1$/s---note the continuous-time units, distinct from the per-attack $r_E$ in the Metabolic Policy section---, $E_{\mathrm{cap}}\!=\!1.0$). Vitality $v$ scales coupling to a 2\,Hz external kick ($K_{\mathrm{base}}\!=\!3.0$; intrinsic frequency $1.8\!\cdot\!2\pi$\,rad/s, $\pm 2\%$ jitter). Three conditions: \emph{vitality} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot v_i$), \emph{uniform} ($k_{\mathrm{eff},i} = K \cdot \bar{v}$), and \emph{control} ($k_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$); 40\,s runs, 20 seeds each.

Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}A shows the group mean PLV over time. Both vitality and uniform conditions synchronize above the control baseline. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}B plots per-agent final PLV against consonance (using stratified pitch assignment to span the full consonance range): in the vitality condition, consonant agents achieve near-unity PLV while dissonant agents remain desynchronized (the dashed curve is a logistic sigmoid fit, grid-search OLS), whereas the control condition shows no such gradient. Figure~\ref{fig:e5-entrainment}C summarizes the Pearson $r$(consonance, PLV) across all seeds. The vitality condition yields a strong positive correlation ($r = 0.75 \pm 0.09$), confirming that consonance position selectively modulates synchronization strength. The uniform condition, despite identical total coupling, shows near-zero correlation ($r \approx 0.0$), isolating the effect to per-agent vitality modulation rather than aggregate coupling magnitude. This experiment is a pathway-isolation assay: it validates one designed spectral-to-temporal bridge, not that rhythmic organization in the full runtime is determined by consonance alone.

\subsection{Experiment~4: Hereditary Adaptation}
\label{sec:e6}

Experiments 1--3 establish selection but not heredity. To test whether lineage-based inheritance can consolidate ecological advantage, we compare two respawn conditions: \emph{heredity}, where a dead agent's replacement pitch is drawn $\mathcal{N}(\text{random surviving parent},\,\sigma\!=\!0.003\,\log_2)$---a minimal inheritance mechanism analogous to cultural transmission rather than genetic encoding---versus \emph{random} uniform respawn within $\pm 1$ octave. Both conditions retain the crowding penalty but disable hill-climbing, isolating hereditary selection as the sole consonance-improvement pathway (remaining parameters in Table~\ref{tab:conditions}). Twenty seeds per condition were run until 4{,}000 deaths ($\approx 125$ turnover cycles).

Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary} shows that heredity runs produce a statistically significant increase in $C_{\text{score}}$ relative to random controls. Welch's $t$-test confirmed higher consonance change in the heredity condition ($\Delta C_{\text{score}} = +0.121 \pm 0.177$ vs.\ $-0.003 \pm 0.027$; $t=3.10$, $p=0.001$, Cohen's $d=0.98$). Final consonance was likewise higher (heredity $0.185 \pm 0.178$ vs.\ random $0.061 \pm 0.018$; $t=3.13$, $p<0.001$). Pitch entropy dropped sharply under heredity ($1.67 \pm 0.46$ vs.\ $3.78 \pm 0.03$), indicating convergence toward specific pitch regions. The large inter-seed variance ($\pm 0.177$) reflects drift-dominated early dynamics: 8 of 20 heredity seeds showed $\Delta C > 0.1$ while 7 remained near baseline, consistent with stochastic founder effects \citep{kimura1983}. The final pitch histogram (Figure~\ref{fig:e6-hereditary}C) shows that heredity runs concentrate near consonant ratios rather than remaining uniformly dispersed. This convergence arises purely from differential survival and inheritance: without hill-climbing, only the metabolic selection pressure identified in Experiment~2 drives the effect. Parameters were selected from a sweep over 15 conditions (Supplementary Table~S2); the reported setting is representative rather than the single best arm. To confirm that the consonance-score difference reflects genuine proximity to just-intonation ratios rather than an artefact of the in-simulation metric, we re-scored final configurations with an independent metric $\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}} = \binom{N}{2}^{-1}\!\sum_{i<j} \max_{p:q}\!\bigl[\tfrac{1}{pq}\,e^{-\alpha\,(\Delta_{ij}-r_{pq})^2}\bigr]$, the mean pairwise Gaussian proximity ($\alpha\!=\!4.0 \times 10^{-4}$\,ct$^{-2}$) to just-intonation ratios $p\!:\!q$ ($p,q \leq 8$), weighted by complexity $1/(pq)$: heredity scored substantially higher ($0.55 \pm 0.13$ vs.\ $0.11 \pm 0.01$; Welch $t=15.3$, $p<10^{-12}$, $d=4.8$).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Integrated Interpretation}

The four experiments form a cumulative argument. Self-organized polyphony (Experiment~1) shows that the landscape admits structured configurations beyond trivial collapse; metabolic selection (Experiment~2) validates that consonance-dependent recharge produces quantifiable survival differentials; entrainment gating (Experiment~3) validates the consonance$\to$vitality$\to$coupling pathway linking the spectral and temporal axes; and hereditary adaptation (Experiment~4) shows that selection pressure, once established, can accumulate across generations via inheritance alone. Each experiment isolates one mechanism; together they show that a psychoacoustic landscape can sustain the core ingredients of an artificial ecology without symbolic encoding. As an integration test, we ran the Experiment~4 protocol under four conditions crossing heredity/random respawn with hill-climbing on/off (20 seeds each). Figure~\ref{fig:e6-integration} shows that combining heredity with hill-climbing yields $C_{\text{score}} = 0.53$, exceeding heredity-only ($0.19$), hill-only ($0.11$), and random ($0.06$). Because all four conditions share the same 20 random seeds, the interaction contrast is computed per-seed (HH$_i$ $-$ H$_i$ $-$ RH$_i$ $+$ R$_i$; mean $0.30$, 95\% CI $[0.20, 0.39]$; $p < 0.001$, sign-flip permutation), confirming that the combination exceeds the sum of individual effects. Repeating all four conditions on a shuffled landscape (Supplementary S7.4) eliminates the interaction entirely (contrast $0.03$, $p = 0.19$), confirming that the synergy requires coherent terrain structure.

\subsection{The Ecological--Aesthetic Duality}

Because the fitness landscape is derived from perceptual consonance, ecological viability and musical coherence are, by design, the same quantity---a structural consequence of DCC. This duality is not merely a design convenience but a falsifiable structural claim: if the psychoacoustic landscape is ecologically functional, then ecological success should correlate with perceptual coherence---a prediction testable by behavioural listening experiments (see Limitations). A potential objection is that unison is ecologically optimal yet aesthetically trivial; the roughness and crowding penalties break this degeneracy (Experiment~1).

\subsection{Terrain Validity}

Because all in-simulation metrics derive from the same psychoacoustic model that defines the fitness landscape, a central question is whether the reported structure depends on the specific properties of that landscape or would arise under any scalar fitness field. To address this circularity, we introduced matched terrain controls (Supplementary~S7).
In a shuffled-landscape control, a fixed random permutation of the consonance-score bins---generated once per run and held fixed throughout all sweeps---was applied, preserving the marginal score distribution while destroying spatial coherence.
Pitch spread remained intact ($19.5$ vs.\ $19.1$ unique bins, $p = 0.46$), indicating that the crowding penalty alone prevents collapse, but interval structure degraded substantially: entropy rose from $3.91 \pm 0.18$ to $4.61 \pm 0.19$\,nats ($p < 0.0001$), approaching the uniform distribution.
Thus, it is not merely the presence of a fitness signal that matters; a coherent psychoacoustic gradient is required for the emergence of structured polyphony.
A coefficient sweep over 16 $(b,c)$ kernel settings confirmed that polyphonic diversity is robust across the grid (Supplementary Table~S6).
The terrain control extends to the integrated system: repeating all four integration-test conditions on a shuffled landscape reduces the heredity$\times$hill-climbing interaction contrast from $0.30$ to $0.03$ ($p = 0.19$; Supplementary S7.4), eliminating the synergy entirely. To test whether the interaction depends on the specific landscape topology, we further repeated the integration test under three environment-partials settings ($3$, $6$, $10$) that produce qualitatively different harmonicity profiles. The interaction contrast remains large and significant across all topologies ($0.30$--$0.37$; $p < 0.001$; Supplementary S7.5), confirming that the synergy generalizes across landscape structures.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limits}

The terrain controls establish that the reported self-organisation---from emergent polyphony (Experiment~1) through the heredity$\times$hill-climbing synergy (Experiment~4)---depends on coherent psychoacoustic topology, not on an arbitrary fitness signal, but they do not address whether emergent configurations predict human aesthetic judgements; behavioural listening tests remain necessary for that separate question. The independent ratio-complexity check ($\mathrm{JI}_{\text{score}}$) provides a model-external probe, confirming the large heredity--random differentiation in Experiment~4 while returning uniformly low scores in Experiment~1. Because harmonicity--roughness weighting varies across listeners and cultures \citep{mcdermott2016}, the present coefficients implicitly encode a Western-leaning profile; listener-informed reparametrisation could enable non-Western perceptual weightings.

Experiment~4's hereditary adaptation converges to fixed-landscape attractors and does not constitute open-ended evolution \citep{bedau2000}; whether evolvable timbres or coupling topologies could support open-ended dynamics remains open. The consonance landscape currently assumes harmonic spectra; in single-anchor environments this renders roughness negligible, reducing the bilinear core to a harmonicity-only signal. Extending to multi-anchor or inharmonic timbres would activate the full $H$--$R$ interaction and test whether DCC generalizes beyond tonal contexts. More broadly, closing the loop via real-time listener biosignals would turn perception and generation into a coupled dynamical system.

\section{Conclusion}

Conchordal presents a psychoacoustic artificial life environment whose design is grounded in Direct Cognitive Coupling. Harmonicity and roughness define a continuous landscape that supports self-organization, selection, consonance-gated entrainment, and hereditary adaptation---core ingredients of artificial ecology instantiated in an auditory medium structured by perceptual law, complementing the spatial and chemical substrates traditionally studied in ALife. Matched terrain controls confirm that these outcomes---including the synergistic interaction between heredity and landscape-guided search---require the specific gradient structure of the psychoacoustic landscape, not merely any fitness signal; topology generalization across three spectral configurations further shows that the synergy is not an artefact of a particular parameterization, establishing DCC-derived consonance as a viable ALife substrate. As an instrument, Conchordal offers composers a generative substrate in which ecological dynamics produce musically coherent material without symbolic specification. More broadly, DCC is not inherently auditory: any creative medium whose perceptual basis admits quantitative modelling---visual, tactile, or kinesthetic---could host an analogous ecology.
